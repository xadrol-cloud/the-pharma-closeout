### [WEEK IN REVIEW — TOP STORY: Viridian FDA Approval Challenges Tepezza]

ALEX: The week's defining story landed Friday afternoon. The FDA approved Viridian Therapeutics' drug for thyroid eye disease — ending Tepezza's run as the only approved TED therapy. For Amgen, this isn't a competitive nuisance. This is the structural break in a multi-billion-dollar franchise that has operated without a single approved rival.

MAYA: The pricing dynamics shift before the prescribing dynamics do — that's the pattern with monopoly breaks in specialty biologics. Payers now have a second anti-IGF-1R antibody to put on the table in formulary discussions. Amgen has never had to defend Tepezza's price against a direct comparator. That conversation starts immediately. And what's critical here is that Amgen doesn't get to set the tempo. Payers set the tempo. The moment a second option exists, the procurement leverage flips — even before a single patient switches.

ALEX: Amgen inherited Tepezza through the Horizon acquisition, and it's been one of their cleanest commercial stories — a product growing in a defined specialty market with no alternative. Viridian doesn't need to capture half the patient population to create problems. They just need to show up in formulary discussions. That's the asymmetry that makes monopoly breaks so damaging — the revenue erosion isn't proportional to share loss. It starts with price concessions on the entire installed base just to maintain access parity, and that compression hits the P&L before a single prescription flips.

MAYA: What shifts at the point of care is subtler but just as important. TED management has been single-source for the entire treatment class, and that shapes prescriber behavior in ways that only surface when the dynamic breaks. When endocrinologists and ophthalmologists suddenly have genuine prescribing choice within the same mechanistic class, the switching-cost architecture that Tepezza has relied on starts to dissolve. Clinicians who've been funneling every patient through one biologic will now evaluate differentiators they didn't need to weigh before. And I'd push back on any framing that reduces this to a payer story alone — the clinical community's comfort with a single-product paradigm has been a competitive moat in its own right, and that moat just got breached.

ALEX: And this didn't happen in isolation. Amgen was absorbing a CHMP recommendation to revoke Tavneos authorization in Europe the same week — we'll get to that shortly. But the combined picture is specialty portfolio pressure on two continents simultaneously. That's the kind of convergence that forces a portfolio-level investor conversation, not just a product-by-product defense. Management teams can message around one headwind. Two concurrent headwinds in distinct geographies and distinct therapeutic areas — that challenges the entire narrative that Amgen's specialty portfolio carries durable competitive insulation.

MAYA: The label details will dictate the pace of share erosion — patient population, dosing schedule, any restrictions Amgen can exploit in competitive messaging. But the structural conclusion is already locked in. The monopoly premium in Amgen's TED franchise ended Friday. That margin compression doesn't reverse. And the strategic implication extends beyond TED — it forces Amgen to re-evaluate how much defensive investment every single-source franchise in the portfolio actually requires, because the Tepezza experience just demonstrated how quickly competitive insulation can evaporate.

### [WEEK IN REVIEW — DEAL LANDSCAPE]

ALEX: The deal activity this week told two sharply different stories about what large pharma is actually buying — and the contrast is worth sitting with. Let's start with the bigger number. Merck KGaA — Darmstadt, not Rahway — paid eleven-point-three billion dollars for Bio-Techne, a life sciences tools and services company. Their largest deal in a decade. Let's bring in Marcus Webb on this one.

MARCUS: Eleven-point-three billion for infrastructure, not molecules. Merck KGaA is buying the reagents, instruments, and bioprocessing capabilities that every drug developer needs regardless of which therapeutic area wins. The thesis is upstream diversification — own the tools, and your revenue correlates with industry-wide R&D spend, not with any single clinical bet. It's a picks-and-shovels play at a premium price. And the strategic logic is deliberately counter-cyclical to the molecule-hunting we see from most large pharma acquirers — Merck KGaA is betting that the aggregate volume of drug development matters more than picking the right drug.

ALEX: Now flip to Biogen, which is running the opposite playbook. After buying Apellis Pharmaceuticals for five-point-six billion dollars, Biogen is cutting most of the Apellis pipeline programs. The read-through: Biogen bought a company for its complement platform and is now stripping it down to a single commercial asset in geographic atrophy. That's a fundamental redefinition of what they thought they were acquiring. It suggests the diligence thesis — that complement biology had broad therapeutic optionality — has been abandoned by the acquirer itself within the integration window.

MAYA: So Syfovre has to carry the entire five-point-six-billion-dollar price tag on its own. No pipeline optionality, no platform expansion — just one product's GA revenue trajectory justifying the acquisition math. That's a very narrow thesis for a very large check. And what makes it particularly precarious is that geographic atrophy is a market where long-term treatment persistence is still being established. If Syfovre's real-world adherence curves come in softer than the trial-based projections, there's no pipeline hedge to absorb the shortfall.

ALEX: It is. And it only works if Syfovre's commercial ramp doesn't stall. The broader financing environment, by contrast, stayed hot all week. The BIO conference spotlighted an IPO class that already includes five of the six largest new biotech stock offerings since the start of 2022. Quick rundown — Kardigan pulled in four hundred million in its IPO. Ollin secured three hundred thirty million for pivotal trials of a Vabysmo challenger out of China. Definium raised eight hundred million through a stock offering. Novartis signed a small molecule discovery pact with Antares — the Scorpion Therapeutics spinout — at a hundred and five million upfront. Osanni Bio closed a hundred and ninety million for ophthalmology and cardiology. Lycia raised seventy-five million for food allergy and Graves' disease. And Oblenio Bio clinched sixty-two million led by Pfizer Ventures for autoimmune programs.

MAYA: The Biogen story is the one that ripples furthest. When the acquirer that just spent five-point-six billion on the leading complement platform decides the pipeline extensions beyond GA aren't worth funding, that reprices complement biology risk for every company working in adjacent programs. The signal isn't just about Biogen — it's about what large pharma believes complement can do outside the eye. If the company that owns the most advanced complement asset in the market has concluded the platform doesn't extend, smaller complement-focused biotechs now face a credibility headwind in every partnering and financing conversation. That chilling effect is the real downstream consequence of Biogen's pipeline cuts.

### [WEEK IN REVIEW — REGULATORY & CLINICAL]

ALEX: Maya, the regulatory docket this week was dense. Give us the headline.

MAYA: The standout was the FDA's expanded approval for Ionis's Tryngolza — per the agency, the first approved treatment to reduce the risk of acute pancreatitis in patients with severe hypertriglyceridemia. That landing matters because of what it does to Ionis as a company, but the clinical nuance is where the real implication sits.

ALEX: Start with the company story. Analysts are calling Tryngolza a potential multibillion-dollar medicine. Ionis has spent its entire history positioned as a platform monetization company — licensing antisense technology to partners, collecting milestones and royalties. A proprietary commercial asset at this scale flips the investment thesis from platform to product company. That's not an incremental change. It's a different valuation framework. The investor base that owns Ionis as a royalty stream is fundamentally different from the investor base that owns a commercial-stage specialty pharma company — and that transition creates both opportunity and turbulence in the shareholder register.

MAYA: Now the clinical piece. This is not a lipid-lowering label. It's a pancreatitis risk-reduction label. The patient identification pathway is completely different — you're finding patients through emergency department presentations and recurrent acute episodes, not through lipid panel screening in primary care. The clinical urgency is immediate, and the cost-offset calculus for payers maps against hospitalization costs, not against statin comparisons. That distinction determines everything about how fast and how broadly this gets adopted. And I want to emphasize — the payer conversation here is actually favorable precisely because of that framing. Preventing ICU admissions for acute pancreatitis is a quantifiable cost-avoidance story. That's a much cleaner reimbursement argument than marginal lipid improvement in a primary care setting.

ALEX: The rest of the regulatory week moved fast. In Europe, the CHMP recommended revoking Tavneos marketing authorization — reversing its own previous endorsement — while separately giving Acadia Pharmaceuticals a positive opinion on its second attempt after prior rejection. The European Commission opened an antitrust probe into Sanofi over alleged disparagement of a rival flu vaccine. And stateside, the FDA accepted Replimune's oncolytic immunotherapy filing, described as the latest signal of shifting attitudes at the agency toward novel modalities. Capricor also has an advisory panel meeting scheduled.

MAYA: The Tavneos reversal demands the extra beat. CHMP doesn't routinely recommend revoking authorization it previously supported. Something in the post-authorization data or the committee's risk-benefit reassessment moved meaningfully. For Amgen, stack that on top of the Viridian approval in the U.S. — losing regulatory ground in Europe and competitive ground domestically in the same five-day window creates a specialty portfolio problem that requires a strategic response, not just a communications plan. And what concerns me from a precedent standpoint is the signal this sends about CHMP's willingness to reverse itself. Companies that have received European approvals in specialty categories with thin evidence packages should be watching this closely — it suggests the committee's post-authorization scrutiny bar may be rising in ways that affect portfolio planning across the industry, not just for Amgen.

### [UNDER THE RADAR]

MAYA: Under the radar this week — and this genuinely got buried by the pharma-specific headlines — the Supreme Court issued a ruling that blunts state-level claims against Bayer's Roundup weedkiller.

ALEX: This reads like an agriculture story, but the pharma implications are real. Bayer has been carrying Roundup litigation exposure like an anchor on the entire company — pharma division included. That overhang has constrained Bayer's pharma business development relative to peers for years. A ruling that limits state-level attack surface directly affects how much capital needs to sit in litigation reserves versus flowing into R&D and dealmaking. And in a year where we just watched Merck KGaA write an eleven-point-three-billion-dollar check, the question of whether Bayer can finally play at that scale in pharma becomes much more concrete if the Roundup liability begins to shrink.

MAYA: It's easy to dismiss as irrelevant if you're watching Bayer purely through the pharma lens. But for a diversified conglomerate, crop science litigation can starve the pharma unit of investment capacity in ways pure-play biotech investors consistently miss. If this ruling holds, Bayer's pharma team may have more room to compete for assets in the next dealmaking cycle than they've had in years. That would meaningfully change the competitive dynamics for mid-cap acquisition targets — another well-capitalized buyer entering the market shifts the entire bid landscape.
