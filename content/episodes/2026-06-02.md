### [TOP STORY — ASCO Data Day: Celcuity, Abivax, Revolution Medicines]

ALEX: Top story — the ASCO data flood, and it's moving markets in both directions. Start with Celcuity. Phase 3 VIKTORIA-1 spotlighted gedatolisib, a pan-PI3K/mTOR inhibitor, in PIK3CA-mutated HR-positive, HER2-negative breast cancer. The headline — gedatolisib roughly doubled PFS compared to Novartis's Piqray. An investigator said the data could establish a new standard of care. Investors disagreed. Earlier testing had shown encouraging median PFS in an initial cohort, setting elevated expectations heading into the full Phase 3 readout. Celcuity says it will seek broad FDA approval on these data.

MAYA: Doubling PFS against an approved comparator in a defined molecular subtype is a meaningful clinical result — full stop. The stock reaction tells you about expectation management, not clinical significance. A small cohort set the bar, and Phase 3 data in broader populations almost never replicate the best-case signal from a concentrated dataset. The question oncologists are going to ask first is not about PFS magnitude — it's the safety profile relative to Piqray. If gedatolisib sidesteps the hyperglycemia burden that limits Piqray tolerability, the standard-of-care argument at tumor boards could be considerably stronger than today's price action suggests.

ALEX: Now Abivax — different dynamic entirely. Obefazimod delivered what's being called landmark efficacy in the Phase 3 ABTECT maintenance trial for ulcerative colitis. Primary endpoint met, all key secondaries met. At interim timepoint, both dose cohorts showed comparable placebo-adjusted clinical remission rates. That's a real signal in a crowded UC landscape. But shares tanked after the company disclosed several cancer cases among treated patients — and that's the overhang.

MAYA: The cases included prostate, breast, and skin cancers, plus colonic dysplasia. Abivax says unrelated to treatment. But here's the adjudication challenge the FDA will face — UC patients already carry elevated baseline colorectal malignancy risk, and colonic dysplasia is actively surveilled in this population precisely because it's premalignant. Disentangling a treatment-emergent signal from the expected background noise in a disease with inherent cancer predisposition is going to be the central regulatory question. Strong efficacy buys you the right to answer it, but it doesn't answer it for you.

ALEX: And then there's Revolution Medicines. Their pancreatic cancer data at ASCO is being described as unprecedented — investigators are using the phrase paradigm shift. Revolution is building RAS-targeted therapies in one of the most RAS-driven tumor types. This is the same company Reuters reported in January was in acquisition talks with Merck at a valuation of 28 to 32 billion dollars. If those discussions are still live, today's data either accelerates the timeline or gives Revolution substantially more leverage at the negotiating table. Akeso and Summit also reported survival extension in a closely watched lung cancer trial — so the broader ASCO theme is targeted therapies delivering in historically resistant settings.

MAYA: The RAS revolution is the throughline. Pancreatic cancer has been one of the most intractable malignancies in oncology for decades — a tumor type where incremental gains are measured in weeks, not months. If Revolution's approach is genuinely bending the survival curve there, the implications reach well beyond one company's acquisition premium. It reframes how the field thinks about drugging what was considered undruggable. That's a multi-company, multi-year inflection.

---

### [DEAL & PIPELINE ROUNDUP]

ALEX: Three ASCO bombshells in one session — but deals didn't stop for the data. Shifting to the deal side. Travere Therapeutics paid Everest Medicines 112.5 million dollars upfront for ex-China rights to civorebrutinib, a BTK inhibitor targeting rare kidney diseases. Total deal economics include substantial upfront payments alongside milestone-based and tiered royalty structures. Travere is building a concentrated rare nephrology portfolio — and this is another cross-border China-to-US licensing structure landing right as new legislation targeting Chinese biotech companies was unveiled today by a bipartisan group of lawmakers. The timing is hard to ignore.

MAYA: The commercial logic works — limited approved options in rare kidney disease, well-characterized patient populations, specialty pharmacy distribution. If civorebrutinib delivers clinically, Travere has assembled a focused portfolio with real pricing power in a corner of medicine most large pharma companies have bypassed entirely. But the geopolitical overhang on China-sourced pipeline deals just got heavier.

ALEX: Legend Biotech dropped its first in vivo CAR-T data today — LB2501, Phase 1, evaluating patients with relapsed or refractory B-cell non-Hodgkin lymphoma across multiple dose levels. The concept eliminates ex vivo cell manufacturing and lymphodepletion. A formal late-breaking oral is set for EHA on June 14th in Stockholm. We've been tracking in vivo CAR-T repricing since Lilly paid up to seven billion for Kelonia's platform earlier this year — Legend entering with actual patient data adds a significant new reference point.

MAYA: Twelve patients is early. But if in vivo CAR-T replicates the efficacy of ex vivo approaches without the manufacturing bottleneck, you're potentially moving cell therapy from a handful of certified academic centers to a dramatically broader delivery model. That doesn't just change treatment access — it rewrites the unit economics of an entire therapeutic modality.

ALEX: A few more to get through quickly. Fulcrum Therapeutics is exploring a sale after the FDA forced discontinuation of its sickle cell drug pociredir on safety concerns. Shares suffered a dramatic selloff in a single session — this just weeks after the CEO had emphasized significant competitive advantages on the Q1 call. Fulcrum enters strategic review without a lead clinical candidate.

MAYA: The lesson there is familiar but brutal — regulatory risk doesn't care about your competitive positioning narrative.

ALEX: Separately, Merck trimmed 88 positions at its New Jersey headquarters, the latest phase of the three billion dollar cost-cutting plan announced last July. The company is also eyeing its COVID antiviral Lagevrio for potential use in the context of an Ebola outbreak — a repurposing play for a drug with a troubled commercial trajectory post-pandemic. Cutting headcount, exploring antiviral repurposing, and reportedly pursuing a 28-to-32-billion-dollar acquisition simultaneously — that's the Keytruda patent cliff playbook in real time.

MAYA: And on financing — NewLimit raised 435 million at a valuation north of three billion for anti-aging, targeting clinical entry in 2027. Contraline closed a 92.5 million dollar Series B for male birth control. Two historically underfunded categories pulling meaningful institutional capital in the same week.

ALEX: NewLimit at three-billion-plus pre-clinic is a pure conviction bet.

MAYA: The money tells you investor appetite for longevity science is real. But the gap between preclinical enthusiasm and human proof-of-concept is where these valuations either compound or correct.

### [REGULATORY WATCH]

MAYA: On the regulatory side — Cingulate received a complete response letter for CTx-1301, its once-daily ADHD treatment. Importantly, this CRL is entirely CMC-related — manufacturing issues only, no clinical safety or efficacy deficiencies cited. The company says it plans a prompt response. Manufacturing CRLs are generally the most resolvable category, but resubmission timeline remains open.

ALEX: Also today — the FDA accepted Roche's NDA for giredestrant in ER-positive early-stage breast cancer with Priority Review. PDUFA date set for November 30th. Per Roche's announcement, giredestrant is the first oral SERD with positive Phase 3 results in the curative setting. But context matters — giredestrant missed in a first-line metastatic trial, and vepdegestrant was approved just last month. The oral SERD landscape is filling up fast.

MAYA: Where giredestrant's label lands in the treatment algorithm determines everything about commercial viability. The early breast cancer opportunity is large — more than 90 percent of ER-positive cases are diagnosed at early stage. But the metastatic miss narrows the total addressable story, and sequencing against vepdegestrant and AstraZeneca's camizestrant will be the competitive battleground. November 30th clarifies a great deal about this class.

ALEX: Two PDUFA dates on the June calendar worth flagging. Merck is awaiting the FDA's decision on WELIREG plus KEYTRUDA as a combination, and Spero Therapeutics has a decision pending on its antibiotic for complicated UTIs. The WELIREG-KEYTRUDA call is the higher-stakes outcome by far.

MAYA: That decision tests whether Merck can build durable combination franchises that extend Keytruda's commercial life through the patent cliff. If the label comes in broad, it becomes a template for future combos — and it signals to investors that the post-cliff strategy has substance behind it, not just cost cuts and bolt-on deals.
