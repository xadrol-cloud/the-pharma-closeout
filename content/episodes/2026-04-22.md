### [TOP STORY — Merck IDVYNSO FDA Approval]

ALEX: The FDA approved Merck's IDVYNSO on Monday — once-daily, single-tablet, a hundred milligrams of doravirine plus zero-point-two-five milligrams of islatravir. Indicated for HIV-1 in adults, replacing their current antiretroviral regimen in virologically suppressed patients with no treatment failure history. The headline is islatravir — the first approved nucleoside reverse transcriptase translocation inhibitor, a first-in-class mechanism per Merck's filing. This is yet another novel drug approval from the FDA this year, and it lands squarely in Gilead's core therapeutic territory.

MAYA: The islatravir backstory matters. Earlier development at higher doses ran into dose-dependent CD4-positive lymphocyte declines — serious enough that Merck shelved the original program entirely and reworked the dosing from scratch. A lot of people in the field wrote this molecule off. Getting it across the finish line at a quarter-milligram dose is a genuine salvage story, and a signal that Merck's clin-pharm team was willing to rebuild the program rather than abandon the mechanism.

ALEX: So the molecule survived. The commercial question is whether it can compete. Gilead has owned HIV therapeutics for years — Biktarvy is deeply entrenched. IDVYNSO gives physicians a two-drug alternative with a novel mechanism, but switching inertia in HIV is enormous. You're asking prescribers to move stable patients off a three-drug regimen backed by years of real-world evidence.

MAYA: The two-drug pitch has theoretical appeal — fewer active agents, potentially lower long-term toxicity burden. But theoretical appeal doesn't move prescriptions. And the label narrows the initial opportunity to suppressed patients with no failure history. Merck is being deliberate about where they enter, but that deliberateness caps the addressable population on day one.

ALEX: I'd push back slightly. The deliberateness is the strategy. Merck isn't trying to unseat Biktarvy head-to-head at launch — they're establishing the platform with the cleanest possible patient population and building real-world evidence from there.

MAYA: That framing is generous. It assumes the expansion path stays open, and it ignores the fact that long-acting injectables are pulling the competitive conversation in a completely different direction. Merck isn't just competing with Gilead's oral portfolio — they're competing with the convenience narrative around every-other-month dosing. A once-daily pill is an upgrade over three-drug regimens but a downgrade versus injectables for patients who can access them.

ALEX: Fair point. But having a novel mechanism in play reintroduces competitive pressure Gilead hasn't faced in years. And if islatravir holds up at scale in post-marketing populations, it becomes the backbone for future combination strategies — that's where Merck's real franchise value sits, not in this single tablet. Shifting to the deal side — IDVYNSO wasn't even Merck's biggest headline today. The company also announced a multi-year partnership with Google Cloud valued at up to one billion dollars — AI infrastructure, embedded Google Cloud engineers working alongside Merck teams, and deployment of the Gemini Enterprise platform across R&D, manufacturing, commercial, and corporate functions. Let's bring in Marcus Webb for the structural read.

### [DEAL & PIPELINE ROUNDUP]

MARCUS: A billion-dollar multi-year AI commitment is not a research collaboration — it's an operating system decision. Merck is wiring Google's infrastructure into core workflows across the entire organization. The real question is whether this produces measurable cycle-time compression in drug development or primarily modernizes enterprise operations. How Merck benchmarks ROI on future earnings calls will tell you which it actually is.

ALEX: Two more deals north of a billion. Amneal Pharmaceuticals agreed to acquire biosimilars specialist Kashiv BioSciences in a cash-and-stock deal worth up to one-point-one billion. And AbbVie is making a substantial investment to build a significant pharmaceutical manufacturing campus in Durham, North Carolina — creating a considerable number of new jobs according to the company. Domestic manufacturing at this scale landing against the hundred-percent pharma tariff backdrop is not a coincidence.

MAYA: On the Amneal deal — owning scaled biosimilar development and manufacturing in-house changes the economics entirely. When you model contract manufacturing costs versus owned capacity across a multi-product biosimilar portfolio, the margin differential over a decade is substantial. This is Amneal positioning for the biologic patent cliff, not just the next product launch.

ALEX: Few more to flag quickly. Tortugas Neuroscience launched with a hundred and six million dollars and a portfolio of brain drugs licensed from Hansoh Pharma and Eisai — led by former Sage Therapeutics executives Jeff Jonas and Al Robichaud. BioAge reported early data suggesting what they called best-in-class potential for an NLRP3 inflammasome-targeting drug, rebuilding their story after an obesity program setback. And Nektar Therapeutics priced a three-hundred-and-twenty-five-million-dollar offering on the back of alopecia extension data showing deepening treatment responses — shares have been on a year-long rally. On the pipeline side, two major readouts. Revolution Medicines reported positive topline results from the Phase 3 RASolute 302 trial — daraxonrasib showed a significant overall survival benefit in previously treated metastatic pancreatic cancer. Revolution's own release described the benefit as unprecedented. The company also priced a two-billion-dollar capital raise as the XBI reached pandemic-era highs. Separately, Bristol Myers Squibb's SUCCESSOR-2 showed mezigdomide with carfilzomib and dex delivering a statistically significant PFS improvement over Kd alone in relapsed or refractory myeloma.

MAYA: An OS benefit in second-line PDAC is extraordinary — full stop. This is a tumor that has been essentially impervious to meaningful survival gains for decades despite enormous investment. If the full AACR presentation holds up to scrutiny, daraxonrasib doesn't just validate Revolution's RAS-targeting platform — it potentially resets the treatment algorithm for one of the deadliest solid tumors we treat.

---

### [REGULATORY WATCH]

ALEX: That Revolution data is the kind of readout regulators notice too. But also worth noting on the regulatory side — a few decisions quietly reshaping competitive dynamics.

MAYA: Starting with myeloma. Sanofi disclosed the FDA extended its review timeline for subcutaneous Sarclisa — pushing back the revised target action date. Sarclisa has been chasing Darzalex for years, and the subQ formulation was supposed to close the convenience gap. In community oncology, infusion chair time directly drives treatment selection — it's tied to reimbursement economics and patient throughput. Every month this decision slips is another month Darzalex's subcutaneous franchise runs uncontested.

ALEX: And Roche filed fenebrutinib with regulators this week — a notably bold move given the safety picture.

MAYA: The efficacy data are clean. FENhance 2 demonstrated that fenebrutinib significantly reduced annualized relapse rates compared to teriflunomide over an extended study period in relapsing MS. But Roche also reported a case of severe liver side effects in the trial. Reuters noted two patient deaths during the program — Roche stated neither was hepatotoxicity-related and that transaminase elevations were comparable to the comparator arm. UBS flagged those deaths as a potential regulatory risk. Filing with that profile is a calculated bet — the advisory committee conversation around hepatic monitoring requirements and label language will determine whether this becomes a broad convenience play or a restricted second-line option.

ALEX: The implications reach well beyond this single molecule. How regulators weigh that liver signal sets the tone for every oral BTK inhibitor program chasing the MS market — there are multiple assets right behind fenebrutinib watching how the FDA responds.

### [WHAT TO WATCH]

ALEX: Looking ahead — AACR twenty-twenty-six continues this week. Revolution Medicines is presenting updated Phase 1/2 data for daraxonrasib in first-line metastatic pancreatic cancer across monotherapy and combination cohorts.

MAYA: That's the presentation that tells you whether daraxonrasib is a second-line story or a full-franchise play across treatment lines. If the first-line data track with the Phase 3 results in pretreated patients, the blockbuster thesis stops being speculative.

ALEX: Also watching ripple effects from Lilly's Kelonia buyout — up to seven billion dollars for an in vivo CAR-T platform, still repricing early-stage cell therapy valuations across the board. That's a thesis under real pressure — and it changes the risk calculus for anyone still holding RIPK assets.
