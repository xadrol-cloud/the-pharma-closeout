### [TOP STORY — Gilead Acquires Arcellx for $7.8 Billion]

ALEX: Top story today. Gilead Sciences has agreed to acquire Arcellx for up to $7.8 billion — $115 per share in cash, plus a five-dollar contingent value right tied to cumulative anito-cel sales reaching six billion by year-end 2029. That's a 68% premium to Arcellx's thirty-day volume-weighted average. Marcus Webb has the deal breakdown.

MARCUS: The centerpiece is anito-cel — next-generation CAR-T for relapsed or refractory multiple myeloma. Arcellx has been co-developing it with Gilead's Kite division since 2023. The BLA is already accepted, action date December 23rd. So Gilead is buying with a regulatory catalyst less than ten months out. Think Celgene acquiring Juno Therapeutics in 2018 — same playbook. Buy the platform before approval, own the manufacturing ramp. The deal structure tells you how they see the risk. $115 per share upfront captures the clinical-stage value. The five-dollar CVR only pays if cumulative sales hit six billion by 2029. That gates the premium to commercial execution, not just approval.

ALEX: So the strategic read is Gilead reinforcing its cell therapy portfolio at scale. Kite's Yescarta franchise has been growing, but the competitive field in multiple myeloma is where the next battleground sits. With Bristol pulling back and J&J expanding, Gilead is betting that owning both the manufacturing expertise and the next clinical entrant gives them a durable second-mover position.

MAYA: I'd push back on the framing that this is purely defensive. Look at the clinical architecture — anito-cel uses a novel binding domain that's structurally distinct from the BCMA-targeted approaches, and the early-phase durability data has been drawing attention in hematology specifically because the response quality profile looks different from what Carvykti delivers. If the differentiation thesis holds through the BLA review, Gilead isn't defending a portfolio — they're buying their way into a differentiated second entry.

ALEX: That's a fair distinction. And if the Phase 3 data confirms what the early signals suggest, the commercial framing shifts from "catching up to Carvykti" to "offering an alternative with a different clinical profile." Either way, Gilead still needs to prove they can manufacture and deliver cell therapy at the volume the myeloma market demands. The competitive read: if you're running oncology commercial strategy and you've been modeling this landscape as Carvykti-dominated, the anito-cel BLA acceptance plus this acquisition structure may reshape your planning assumptions for 2027.

MAYA: One more layer — the CVR benchmark of six billion in cumulative sales by 2029 is aggressive. That's not a safety net, that's a statement about commercial ambition. If Gilead hits it, the implied peak revenue trajectory would put anito-cel among the highest-performing cell therapy launches on record. The deal price only makes sense if they believe the market is expanding, not just redistributing share.

---

### [DEAL & PIPELINE ROUNDUP]

ALEX: Shifting to the deal side with the fuller breakdown on a major clinical readout. We flagged the EV-304 data in Sunday's Under the Radar — the full numbers deserve attention. Pfizer and Astellas published results from the Phase 3 trial testing PADCEV plus Keytruda as perioperative therapy in cisplatin-eligible muscle-invasive bladder cancer.

MAYA: The complete dataset is strong across every dimension. A 47% reduction in the risk of recurrence or death versus standard neoadjuvant chemotherapy. Pathological complete response hit 55.8% compared with 32.5% for chemo. And overall survival showed a 35% reduced risk of death. That OS signal is the number that changes the conversation — pCR is a strong surrogate, but overall survival in the perioperative setting is the data payers and guideline committees require.

ALEX: That makes PADCEV plus Keytruda the first platinum-free perioperative regimen to show both event-free and overall survival benefit in this setting. The durability signal — estimated 79% of patients event-free at two years — is already strong enough to shift treatment algorithm conversations at the next GU ASCO session.

MAYA: And the pCR rate is the number that extends beyond bladder cancer. ADC-checkpoint combinations displacing platinum-based chemotherapy in the curative setting — not just metastatic disease — is a proof-of-concept every solid tumor program running an ADC-IO perioperative trial just got a reference point for.

ALEX: Also worth noting — the CagriSema analyst reaction we covered through the week deepened further. Novo's shares fell over 16% when markets opened on February 24th following the REDEFINE 4 announcement, with additional losses through the week — the stock is now down roughly 60% from its peak. At least seven analyst downgrades followed. Barclays cut peak sales estimates from twelve billion to two billion — an 80% haircut.

MAYA: That Barclays revision is the one that tells the real story. They're not saying CagriSema fails — they're saying the commercial model without a weight loss parity argument can't support a twelve-billion-dollar peak. Novo's differentiation thesis now lives entirely on cardiovascular outcomes and lean mass data that hasn't read out yet. The market is repricing the probability of that data arriving in time to shift launch positioning.

---

### [REGULATORY WATCH]

MAYA: Both FDA decisions we flagged Friday are now resolved. Starting with the bigger commercial read: Ascendis Pharma's navepegritide, branded YUVIWEL, received accelerated approval last Friday for achondroplasia in children aged two and older. First once-weekly treatment in this space, competing against BioMarin's daily-dosed Voxzogo. Ascendis also picked up a Rare Pediatric Disease Priority Review Voucher. Stock moved up about 3%, with Cantor raising their target to 300 and Stifel to 325.

ALEX: The accelerated approval pathway is the piece to parse. Confirmatory trial obligations will define how aggressively Ascendis can compete on market share while the post-marketing data matures — and BioMarin's response on pricing or indication expansion becomes the next competitive signal.

MAYA: And the Rare Pediatric Disease voucher is worth tracking separately — those have been trading in the $100 to $150 million range. For a company Ascendis's size, that's not a rounding error. It materially changes the cash position heading into the commercial launch.

ALEX: Second decision — Dupixent was approved for AFRS on February 27th — first medication ever indicated specifically for allergic fungal rhinosinusitis. The Phase 3 data showed a 92% reduction in risk of subsequent sinus surgery. Another incremental TAM expansion that deepens the payer conversation across the entire ENT corridor for the Regeneron-Sanofi franchise.

ALEX: And on the March regulatory calendar, Rhythm Pharmaceuticals submitted additional Phase 3 TRANSCEND data to the FDA yesterday. Imcivree in acquired hypothalamic obesity. The expanded dataset showed 18.8% placebo-adjusted BMI reduction at 52 weeks across 142 patients. The FDA specifically requested these sensitivity analyses back in October. Action date is March 20th.

MAYA: That's the most commercially significant FDA decision this month. If approved, Imcivree moves from ultra-rare genetic obesity into an acquired-disease framework that analysts project as a billion-dollar-plus opportunity. And the regulatory precedent matters — the FDA's willingness to accept an expanded indication here signals how they're thinking about mechanism-defined obesity subtypes. The implications for MC4 receptor agonists as a class extend well beyond Rhythm's own pipeline.
