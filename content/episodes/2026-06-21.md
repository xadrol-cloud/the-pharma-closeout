### [WEEK IN REVIEW — TOP STORY: Moderna mRNA Flu Vaccine Advisory Panel]

ALEX: Week in review, and the top story is Moderna. On Thursday, an FDA advisory committee voted unanimously to endorse Moderna's mRNA flu vaccine. Unanimously — every member of the panel. This is a program the FDA controversially declined to even review earlier this year. The agency's own staff scrutinized the evidence heading into Thursday's meeting and highlighted what they characterized as shortcomings in the data package. And the panel heard all of that and still voted yes across the board.

MAYA: The instinct is to read this as a Moderna redemption arc, and the stock will probably trade like one on Monday. The sharper question is what shifted inside the FDA. Review staff entered Thursday with a genuinely skeptical written assessment, critiquing sufficiency of the evidence, and the committee overrode that framing completely. When you see that kind of daylight between staff position and committee vote, it typically means the panel weighted the immunogenicity correlate as a clinically adequate surrogate even when the randomized efficacy dataset didn't meet the reviewers' preferred evidentiary standard. That's a methodological precedent with implications well beyond a single product.

ALEX: From a portfolio standpoint, this puts Moderna on track for a potential second major commercial product. The COVID franchise has been in decline, and the entire investment thesis hinges on whether mRNA can become a multi-product platform or whether it remains a one-pandemic story. The company has been spending against that vision — R&D investment, manufacturing capacity, commercial build-out — without a second product to validate it. An approved flu vaccine delivers recurring seasonal revenue and proves the technology works for routine immunization, not just emergency pandemic response. Thursday was the most tangible evidence yet that the thesis might hold.

MAYA: Getting approval is where this starts, not where it ends. Moderna would be entering a market where Sanofi and CSL have decades of real-world effectiveness data behind their flu franchises. Formulary committees will want to see head-to-head differentiation, not just mechanism novelty. And the pricing conversation in seasonal vaccines is nothing like the latitude that COVID afforded.

ALEX: The commercial barriers are real. But a unanimous panel vote hands Moderna leverage in those formulary conversations that didn't exist six months ago. And the timeline compounds this. Flu vaccine manufacturing decisions for the upcoming season lock in months ahead. If the FDA doesn't act quickly on the approval itself, the commercial impact slides a full year.

MAYA: There's a subtler institutional dynamic worth tracking. FDA review staff flagged concerns publicly, and the committee dismissed them unanimously. That kind of outcome can recalibrate how staff approach future mRNA filings. If your written concerns get overridden at the panel stage, does that change how conservative your next briefing document reads? The institutional ripple from Thursday's vote could outlast the Moderna-specific outcome.

ALEX: And the platform read-through extends past Moderna. mRNA earning a clean unanimous endorsement for a routine seasonal vaccine — not an emergency pandemic product — clears a credibility bar that reaches to RSV, combination vaccines, and the full downstream pipeline. Every company operating a legacy flu franchise now has a reason to update its forward models. If I'm sitting in competitive intelligence at Sanofi or CSL right now, Monday morning starts with a revised threat assessment.

### [WEEK IN REVIEW — DEAL LANDSCAPE]

ALEX: On the deal side this week, the headline number belongs to Jazz Pharmaceuticals and AbCellera. Up to four point one billion dollars for a collaboration on T cell engagers targeting GI cancers. Let's bring in Marcus Webb on this one.

MARCUS: Four point one billion in total value signals serious conviction that T cell engagers can translate into solid tumors, which has been one of the hardest problems in immuno-oncology. These agents have delivered in blood cancers, but extending that efficacy into solid tumor settings has been a grind. Jazz is betting AbCellera's antibody discovery platform can crack GI malignancies specifically. That price tag tells you this isn't exploratory — it's a strategic franchise buildout.

ALEX: Separately, Biogen acquired the anti-inflammatory startup RayThera in a deal worth up to one billion dollars. RayThera had been operating deep in stealth, which makes the valuation all the more striking. This continues Biogen's systematic push beyond neuroscience into immunology, a pivot that's gained urgency as the legacy MS portfolio faces sustained headwinds. The strategic question is whether immunology can become a genuine growth engine — a real second pillar — or whether it ends up as a hedge while the core business continues to erode.

MAYA: A billion dollars for a company nobody outside a handful of due diligence rooms had even heard of. That means the preclinical or early clinical data was compelling enough to justify the premium purely on speed. What connects this week's deal activity is a single calculus: building a pipeline organically now costs more in time and missed opportunity than paying up for someone else's clinical-stage work.

ALEX: Lilly reinforced that logic by acquiring non-opioid pain drugmaker 4E Therapeutics — the latest in a string of deals that has exceeded eighteen billion dollars over the past few years. It's become Lilly's default operating rhythm. On the startup side, Vedana Therapeutics emerged from stealth with forty-six million dollars for anti-PACAP migraine therapies backed by Westlake, and cAMPfield launched with a hundred and eighty million targeting gut disease drugs. Both entering therapeutic areas where the existing standard of care leaves clear room for improvement.

MAYA: Migraine prevention and GI disease — two spaces where the treatment architecture is genuinely ripe for disruption. Experienced investors putting that kind of early capital behind both signals more than optimism. These are bets on pathways where they've seen enough biology to underwrite the clinical risk, not speculative platform plays chasing a trend.

ALEX: And on the equity side, Kardigan raised four hundred million in its IPO. Per BioPharma Dive data, the 2026 class now includes five of the six largest new biotech stock offerings since the start of 2022. That capital window is wide open, and the pace of issuance tells you companies filing aren't counting on it staying that way. When the window tightens, it tends to tighten fast.

### [WEEK IN REVIEW — REGULATORY & CLINICAL]

MAYA: Capital markets are sending one signal. On the regulatory side, the FDA sent a few of its own this week. The agency approved GSK and Spero Therapeutics' oral treatment for complicated urinary tract infections on Tuesday. For antimicrobial stewardship programs, an oral option that can meaningfully reduce IV courses in cUTI changes how these infections get managed at the practice level — a shift in the actual prescribing workflow, not just an incremental label addition.

ALEX: Merck's Capvaxive picked up a label expansion Thursday. On the enforcement side, the FTC required Aurobindo to divest four drugs to complete its Lannett buyout — a reminder that generic consolidation continues to draw active antitrust scrutiny. And then there's the story moving in the opposite direction: Neumora Therapeutics shut down its depression program. An ARCH-launched company with serious venture pedigree walking away from a core asset.

MAYA: Neumora stings. Depression drug development has a clinical failure rate that humbles even well-capitalized programs. The bar for demonstrating meaningful differentiation against established therapies — including generics that cost almost nothing — has become nearly impossible to clear using conventional trial designs and standard symptom-scale endpoints. The question isn't whether depression biology is valid. It's whether the current clinical development model can capture a treatment effect cleanly enough to differentiate at all. Anyone running a CNS mood disorder program should be revisiting their endpoint strategy.

ALEX: Edgewise also reported results on its cardiac program this week. The drug passed a key trial milestone, but the data left somewhat unclear how it separates from existing therapeutic options. Positive enough to advance, ambiguous enough to keep analysts asking questions about where this fits in the treatment hierarchy.

MAYA: The collective message across this week's clinical landscape held: differentiation is the price of admission, and it's getting steeper. Programs that demonstrated it clearly were rewarded. Anything short of that drew skepticism from both the FDA and the market.

### [UNDER THE RADAR]

MAYA: Under the radar this week, and this one sits in territory that's genuinely unusual for pharma. Hackers claimed to have breached Novo Nordisk and obtained proprietary drug formulations and AI models. They demanded twenty-five million dollars in ransom. Novo reportedly refused to pay. The hackers now say the data is for sale.

ALEX: This is worth sitting with for a moment. Proprietary formulations and AI models represent trade secret value that lives entirely outside patent protection. Patents disclose composition of matter by design — that's the social bargain. The moat for a company like Novo sits in the process engineering, the optimization algorithms, the manufacturing know-how that turns a known molecule into a commercial product at scale. If authentic data is now circulating, there is no recall mechanism. That exposure is permanent.

MAYA: The ransom figure itself is revealing. Twenty-five million is trivial relative to Novo's market cap. The hackers weren't pricing for what the data is worth to Novo. They were pricing for attention — and they got it.

ALEX: For an industry rapidly building proprietary AI infrastructure as a core advantage, this is an early and highly visible stress test. Novo's decision not to pay sets a precedent, but it also means the data may be accessible to anyone willing to look. The implications reach across the entire sector's approach to digital IP protection, and most pharma companies are simply not built for this category of threat.
