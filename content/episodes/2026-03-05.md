### [TOP STORY — THERAVANCE PHASE 3 FAILURE AND RESTRUCTURING]

ALEX: Top story. Theravance Biopharma reported that its Phase 3 CYPRESS study of ampreloxetine in neurogenic orthostatic hypotension did not meet its primary endpoint. The stock fell 26 percent on the news — from $18.95 to $13.96 — and the company announced a complete wind-down of its R&D organization within 48 hours of the readout.

MAYA: The clinical details matter here. Ampreloxetine is a selective norepinephrine reuptake inhibitor designed to treat symptomatic nOH — a chronic drop in blood pressure when standing, caused by insufficient norepinephrine release. The CYPRESS trial enrolled 102 patients and measured change in the Orthostatic Hypotension Symptom Assessment score at week 8. It missed. For a mechanism that Theravance had been developing for years as its lead clinical asset, a miss on a subjective symptom composite endpoint in a small rare disease trial is not entirely surprising in hindsight — these are some of the hardest trials to power and execute.

ALEX: The company response tells you everything about the asset concentration risk. Within 48 hours, Theravance announced it would cut 50 percent of its workforce, cease all R&D activity, and reduce cash burn by 60 percent — approximately $70 million in annual savings through the third quarter. A strategic review committee is now evaluating all options, including a potential sale. What remains is Yupelri, the nebulized bronchodilator already approved for COPD maintenance therapy, which becomes the sole commercial asset sustaining the company.

MAYA: This is a textbook case of what single-asset biotech risk looks like in late-stage development. Theravance had effectively bet the company on ampreloxetine. When that asset failed, there was no second program to absorb the blow, no diversified pipeline to offer shareholders a reason to hold. The 26 percent stock decline is actually moderate compared to some single-asset biotech failures — it reflects the floor value of Yupelri's COPD revenue, which the market is pricing as the surviving asset. But the R&D organization is gone. The scientific team is gone. What's left is a commercial shell managing one product while exploring strategic alternatives.

ALEX: For investors and commercial strategists watching the rare disease space, there's a broader signal here about trial design in conditions with subjective endpoints. Neurogenic orthostatic hypotension is a real clinical need — patients suffer significant functional impairment. But the symptom assessment tools available for endpoint measurement introduce variability that makes small trials particularly vulnerable. The CYPRESS trial enrolled 102 patients. Contrast that with GLP-1 trials enrolling thousands. When your sample size is in the low hundreds and your endpoint is symptom-reported, the margin for error on powering and enrichment is razor-thin.

### [SECONDARY STORY — SCIENCE CORP $230M FOR PRIMA RETINAL IMPLANT]

MAYA: In funding news — Science Corporation closed a $230 million Series C financing round at a post-money valuation of $1.5 billion. The capital will accelerate commercialization of PRIMA, a brain-computer interface retinal implant designed to restore functional vision in patients with advanced macular degeneration.

ALEX: The technology is worth understanding precisely. PRIMA is a chip smaller than a grain of rice that's implanted in the eye. It works in tandem with camera-equipped glasses: the cameras capture visual information, convert it into light patterns, and project those patterns onto the implanted chip. The chip then stimulates surviving retinal cells, routing visual signals through the optic nerve to the brain. The peer-reviewed data published in the New England Journal of Medicine showed that 80 percent of patients demonstrated meaningful improvement in visual acuity — patients were able to read letters, numbers, and words.

MAYA: The regulatory and commercial timeline is what makes this story relevant to our audience today. Science Corp has submitted a CE mark application to the European Union and expects approval by mid-2026. If that timeline holds, PRIMA would be the first brain-computer interface product to reach commercial market — ahead of Neuralink, ahead of Synchron, ahead of every other BCI company currently in development. The company was founded by former Neuralink engineers, and it's now expanding its clinical program beyond macular degeneration into Stargardt disease and retinitis pigmentosa — inherited retinal conditions that cause vision loss in young adults.

ALEX: A $1.5 billion valuation for a pre-commercial BCI company is a significant signal about how the investment community is pricing the intersection of neurotechnology and ophthalmology. For pharma companies with retinal disease franchises — Roche through Genentech, Regeneron, Apellis — PRIMA represents a potential platform shift: from chronic anti-VEGF injections and complement inhibitors to surgical implantation of a device that bypasses diseased photoreceptors entirely. That's a different competitive paradigm than molecule-versus-molecule, and it's worth tracking as PRIMA moves toward commercialization.

### [DEAL & PIPELINE ROUNDUP — ROCHE'S SOUTH KOREA BET]

ALEX: Shifting to the deal roundup. Roche announced a five-year, $484 million investment commitment to South Korea's biopharma ecosystem. The company signed a memorandum of understanding with the Korean Ministry of Health and Welfare to conduct global clinical trials in the country, support R&D talent development, and identify promising biotech firms through open innovation programs.

MAYA: The scale matters in context. Global pharma investment in South Korea increased 74 percent between 2020 and 2024, from $410 million to $712 million annually. Roche's $484 million over five years effectively doubles down on a trend that's already reshaping where global clinical trials are conducted. South Korea's appeal is a combination of a well-characterized patient population, strong regulatory infrastructure through MFDS, and academic medical centers that can recruit at pace. For a company like Roche, which runs hundreds of concurrent global trials, having an established clinical trial ecosystem in East Asia reduces enrollment timelines and diversifies the geographic risk that became painfully apparent during COVID when site access varied dramatically by country.

ALEX: The open innovation component is the piece that's most strategically interesting. Roche is explicitly structuring the investment to identify and partner with Korean biotechs — which means this isn't just about running trials faster. It's about early access to Korean drug development assets before they reach the licensing market. South Korea has produced several globally significant biotech companies in the last five years, particularly in biosimilars and cell therapy. Roche is positioning itself as the preferred partner before AbbVie, Novartis, or Pfizer secure those relationships.

### [REGULATORY BRIEF]

MAYA: On the regulatory calendar — tomorrow is the day. March 6th, the Sotyktu PDUFA for psoriatic arthritis. We've covered the clinical data across multiple episodes this week — 54.2 percent ACR20 at week 16 versus 34.1 percent placebo, TYK2 mechanism, clean safety profile versus the pan-JAK class. The Spherix survey showing one in three rheumatologists viewing it as a substantial advance. At this point, the question isn't whether the data support approval — the POETYK PsA-1 and PsA-2 trials both met their primary endpoints with statistical significance. The question is label language: specifically, how the FDA characterizes TYK2 selectivity relative to JAK inhibitors in the prescribing information, and whether the label includes any comparative safety language that BMS can use in commercial positioning against tofacitinib and baricitinib.

ALEX: Also on March 6th — Lantheus has a PDUFA for a new formulation of its F18 PSMA-targeted PET imaging agent for prostate cancer detection. Smaller story commercially, but Lantheus has been building a significant diagnostics franchise in precision oncology imaging, and this expands the PSMA portfolio. Beyond tomorrow, the March regulatory calendar includes Imcivree on March 20th, linerixibat on March 24th, and Kresladi on March 28th. It's a dense month.
