### [TOP STORY]

ALEX: Eli Lilly is escalating its war against compounded GLP-1s. The company issued a warning that compounded versions of tirzepatide — the active ingredient in Mounjaro and Zepbound — may be dangerous and carry, quote, "unknown risks."

MAYA: They're not stopping at warnings. Lilly says internal testing detected an impurity in certain compounded forms. They've formally requested FDA intervention.

ALEX: Here's the context that matters. Compounding pharmacies captured significant market share during the Zepbound shortages — they filled the gap when Lilly couldn't meet demand. Supply has stabilized. Lilly wants that volume back.

MAYA: The impurity claim is the weapon. But notice what's missing — Lilly hasn't disclosed what the impurity is, what concentration, or whether it's clinically meaningful. They're asking prescribers and patients to trust them.

ALEX: Which puts the FDA in an impossible position. Compounding lives in a regulatory gray zone. 503A and 503B facilities have different oversight, enforcement has been inconsistent for years.

MAYA: If the FDA moves aggressively, they validate Lilly's claims without independent verification. If they don't move, they look like they're protecting compounders over patient safety.

ALEX: No clean answer.

MAYA: None.

ALEX: The commercial stakes are massive. Zepbound is tracking toward blockbuster status, and every prescription that goes to a compounder is revenue Lilly doesn't see. Endpoints characterized this as part of how compounders "reshaped the drug industry." Now that reshaping is being contested.

MAYA: And the precedent here extends well beyond obesity. If Lilly succeeds in forcing FDA action, anyone facing gray-market erosion on a high-demand injectable now has a playbook.

### [DEAL & PIPELINE ROUNDUP]

MAYA: Both with underwriters' options fully exercised. Demand exceeded allocation. The IPO window is shaky, but late-stage biotechs with clinical data can still raise real money.

MAYA: That's ecosystem infrastructure disappearing. Early-stage companies feel it when a major pharma pulls back services like that.

ALEX: The personnel news that caught my attention — BioNTech's co-founders, Ugur Sahin and Özlem Türeci, are stepping down. They're launching a new mRNA company. The couple who built BioNTech through COVID will helm an as-yet-unnamed venture focused on mRNA discovery.
