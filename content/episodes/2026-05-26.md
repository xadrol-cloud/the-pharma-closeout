### [TOP STORY — Eli Lilly Vaccine Trifecta]

ALEX: This morning, Eli Lilly announced agreements to acquire three vaccine-focused biotechs — Seattle-area Curevo, Swiss clinical-stage company LimmaTech Biologics, and Vaccine Company Inc. Combined deal value: up to approximately three point eight billion dollars, per BioSpace. Targets include programs against shingles and Epstein-Barr virus. And this follows Lilly's recent hire of Peter Marks, the former FDA vaccine regulator, who now looks less like a consulting arrangement and more like a founding charter for an entirely new business unit.

MAYA: Lilly has zero vaccine infrastructure and no development history in infectious disease. Building a vaccines division from scratch through three simultaneous acquisitions — the ambition is unmistakable, but the distance between clinical-stage assets and manufactured, distributed vaccines at commercial scale is enormous. That gap doesn't close with a press release.

ALEX: No question on the execution risk. But the strategic calculus is rational. Lilly's core revenue narrative lives in GLP-1 and obesity, and every dollar of that franchise eventually faces patent exposure. The lesson of this M&A cycle is that diversification needs a running start — years before the cliff arrives. Vaccines generate recurring revenue at massive population scale. Look at what shingles alone has done for GSK's top line. And this comes in a year where Q1 biotech M&A activity has already reached record levels. The entire industry is in acquisition mode.

MAYA: That still leaves the hard part. Vaccines require dedicated fill-finish manufacturing, cold chain logistics, and public health agency relationships Lilly simply doesn't possess. Hiring Peter Marks signals regulatory expertise. It doesn't signal operational capacity.

ALEX: Let's bring in Marcus Webb for the structural read on this one.

MARCUS: Three simultaneous acquisitions to enter a brand-new therapeutic area — that's not tuck-in M&A. That's a full declaration of strategic intent. Lilly is telling the market it sees itself as a diversified biopharma company, not a metabolics franchise with oncology attached. The closest structural parallel is Pfizer's pandemic-era vaccine build-out, but Pfizer had decades of institutional vaccine experience through Prevnar and its legacy portfolio. Lilly is starting from a fundamentally different baseline.

ALEX: And the market's initial verdict is telling. Lilly shares held essentially flat, while biotech as a subsector showed modest gains. That's Lilly-specific skepticism. Investors see pipeline-stage risk, not near-term value creation.

MAYA: Which means the burden of proof lands squarely on execution. Whether Peter Marks can translate regulatory credibility into a functioning vaccine platform will determine if this is a genuine strategic inflection — or a three-point-eight-billion-dollar science experiment.

### [DEAL & PIPELINE ROUNDUP]

ALEX: Staying with Lilly — the vaccine deals weren't the only Lilly headline over the holiday weekend. Phase 1b data dropped for VERVE-102, the cardiovascular base-editing therapy Lilly acquired from Verve Therapeutics. Per the company's release, a single infusion delivered substantial reductions in both PCSK9 and LDL cholesterol, with those reductions sustained over an extended duration. No safety signals reported in this data cut.

MAYA: Eighty-eight percent PCSK9 knockdown from a single infusion — that approaches the pharmacodynamic ceiling you'd expect from near-complete allelic disruption. If that durability holds in larger and more heterogeneous cohorts, the entire chronic injectable PCSK9 inhibitor franchise faces a fundamental question about its long-term commercial rationale.

ALEX: Shifting to the deal side — radiopharmaceuticals continue to consolidate. Lantheus Holdings is reportedly weighing a potential seven-billion-dollar buyout proposal from Curium, and Niowave broke ground on a seventy-five-million-dollar radioisotope production facility — a direct bet on the supply chain bottleneck that's constrained the radioligand therapy space.

MAYA: Supply-side investment at that scale says the bet isn't just on one marketed product anymore — it's on an entire therapeutic class. You don't build seventy-five-million-dollar production infrastructure for a single drug's demand curve.

ALEX: Quick hits: Astellas laid out a multi-year plan targeting substantial cumulative annual savings through the end of the decade, while aiming to significantly grow revenue from key medicines including Padcev. Kardigan is readying a Nasdaq IPO to back three cardiovascular drugs. Bristol Myers deepened its AI footprint with a new Anthropic collaboration. And AbbVie trimmed a significant number of positions from its Allergan workforce.

MAYA: Astellas is engineering a controlled restructuring around the Xtandi patent cliff. Padcev carries enormous weight in that five-year plan — whether it and its expanding label can absorb the revenue loss when Xtandi goes generic shapes the entire company's trajectory through 2030.

ALEX: One more data point before we pivot: the healthcare sector overall is up just a third of a percent year-to-date versus nine percent for the S&P 500 — significant underperformance. But biotech as a subsector is showing meaningful relative strength. The money is rotating toward pipeline stories, not established franchises.

### [REGULATORY WATCH]

MAYA: On the regulatory front — Gilead's Hepcludex, bulevirtide, has won FDA approval for chronic hepatitis delta virus infection in adults without cirrhosis or with compensated cirrhosis. This is a drug the FDA previously rejected, which makes the reversal all the more notable.

ALEX: Gilead shares barely moved in trading, showing minimal decline. But the clinical need here is real. Hepatitis delta has had essentially no targeted treatment options in the U.S.

MAYA: And the commercial path is about as clean as it gets in rare disease. Hepatitis D co-occurs exclusively with hepatitis B, so the patient population is small but precisely identifiable and severely underserved. Gilead already has deep hepatologist relationships from decades of hep B and hep C commercialization. The call points are built. This is a small market that's theirs to own from launch.

ALEX: Also worth noting on the regulatory side — Outlook Therapeutics is claiming the FDA will sign off on its bevacizumab biosimilar for wet AMD, this after three Complete Response Letters. And internationally, Braftovi continues to pick up colorectal cancer regulatory wins abroad. We've previously flagged the NCCN guideline pathway as the downstream catalyst to watch there.

MAYA: On Outlook — three CRLs earns serious skepticism until there's an approval letter in hand. On Braftovi, survival data accumulating in BRAF V600E mutant metastatic colorectal cancer is exactly the evidence base that moves treatment guidelines — and guideline incorporation is where prescribing behavior actually changes at the community oncology level.

### [WHAT TO WATCH]

ALEX: Looking ahead — the COINS Act debate is accelerating. The push to add biotechnology to outbound investment restrictions targeting China is intensifying, and the BMS-Hengrui deal — fifteen point two billion dollars covering more than a dozen early-stage drugs — has become the political flashpoint. The GOP chairman of the China panel called that deal out by name last week.

MAYA: The ripple effects go well beyond BMS. MAYA: For oncology commercial teams, the next two weeks are where competitive landscapes get redrawn. The ASCO data wave doesn't just produce headlines — it sets market access strategy and launch sequencing for the back half of the year.
