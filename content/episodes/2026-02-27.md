### [TOP STORY — CagriSema REDEFINE 4]

ALEX: Top story of the week. On Sunday, Novo Nordisk released headline results from REDEFINE 4 — the open-label Phase 3 trial that put CagriSema directly against Lilly's Zepbound in people living with obesity. CagriSema delivered 23% weight loss at 84 weeks. Zepbound hit 25.5%. CagriSema did not meet the primary endpoint of non-inferiority to tirzepatide. In the secondary real-world analysis, the gap held — 20.2% versus 23.6%.

MAYA: Let's unpack what's actually meaningful here, because the headline writes itself as a clean Novo loss and that framing misses the clinical reality entirely. The FDA submission was filed in December — based on REDEFINE 1 and REDEFINE 2, not this trial. REDEFINE 4 was a voluntary move. Novo chose to run this head-to-head before they were required to, which tells you something about how confident they were going in.

ALEX: And twenty-three percent weight loss is still a strong result in absolute terms. The competitive problem isn't the number — it's that Zepbound has now set a visible high-water mark, and every commercial conversation about CagriSema will happen in that shadow unless Novo builds a differentiation story that doesn't rely on weight loss parity.

MAYA: Here's the clinical observation most of the coverage is missing: REDEFINE 4 was an open-label design. That limits the evidentiary weight of any patient-reported outcomes — tolerability, quality of life, treatment satisfaction. When Novo eventually makes label claims on those dimensions, the arguments that matter will have to come from the blinded trials. The open-label data will get scrutinized, and it should.

ALEX: Which is why the broader REDEFINE program still has real work to do. The cardiovascular outcome data and lean mass preservation profile are where CagriSema's differentiation argument needs to live. A higher-dose trial is planned for the second half of this year. REDEFINE 11 data is targeted for early 2027. The story isn't closed — it's just been reframed.

MAYA: Novo's stock reaction said the same thing. A meaningful hit, not a free fall. The market read this as commercial headwind for the launch, not program failure. That's the right calibration.

ALEX: The competitive read: if you're running obesity commercial strategy and you've been tracking CagriSema as a hedge against tirzepatide dominance, REDEFINE 4 didn't close the door — it shifted where the argument has to be made. Weight loss headline parity is off the table. Outcomes differentiation and tolerability are the only paths left that build a durable positioning. Novo has program data still coming that could make that case. The burden of proof just moved.

### [DEAL & PIPELINE ROUNDUP]

MAYA: Shifting to the deal side. GSK made a significant move this week — Marcus has the breakdown.

MARCUS: GSK has agreed to acquire 35Pharma for $950 million in cash. The asset is HS235, an activin signaling inhibitor in early clinical development targeting pulmonary arterial hypertension and pulmonary hypertension due to heart failure with preserved ejection fraction. Phase 1 healthy volunteer data is complete. Patient trials start imminently. No milestones disclosed — this is $950 million upfront for a drug that has never been in a patient. That premium tells you everything about how much the activin mechanism is commanding in the current PH market.

ALEX: The competitive framing is explicit. Merck's Winrevair hit $1.4 billion in sales last year. GSK is forecasting the pulmonary hypertension market at $18 billion by 2032, with activin signaling inhibitors capturing a significant share. They're not waiting for Phase 2 data to de-risk the asset and watch the price go up.

MAYA: Smart read on the optionality. The question HS235's first patient data will answer is whether the activin mechanism is a one-drug story or a platform — and GSK would rather own that answer than be the acquirer trying to get in after it's known.

ALEX: Also worth noting: China-originated licensing deals are running at record pace. Seventeen deals involving China-discovered drugs already in 2026 — ahead of last year's record rate at this point in the calendar. A third of the industry's licensing spend in 2025 went to Chinese biotech partnerships, and that trajectory is accelerating.

MAYA: And on the Phase 3 data front — Lilly published ACHIEVE-3 in The Lancet this week. First head-to-head of orforglipron, their oral small molecule GLP-1, against oral semaglutide in type 2 diabetes. Orforglipron 36 milligrams got 31.4% of patients to below 5.7% A1C. Semaglutide got 11.7% there. Weight loss: 19.7 pounds versus 11. On every primary and secondary endpoint, Lilly won convincingly.

ALEX: The one number that won't let this story settle cleanly: discontinuation at the high dose ran 9.7% for orforglipron versus 4.9% for oral semaglutide. Lilly has submissions planned for later this year, and that gap will be part of every formulary negotiation before real-world persistence data exists. The deal and clinical landscape heading into this weekend is active — balance sheets are armed, the M&A appetite is running hot, and whoever moves next in pulmonary hypertension or oral GLP-1 will be doing it with today's FDA decisions as pricing context.

### [REGULATORY WATCH]

MAYA: Both FDA decisions are landing today. Leading with the more complex read: Ascendis Pharma's navepegritide — TransCon CNP — for achondroplasia. Once-weekly subcutaneous injection in a pediatric rare disease population. Competing directly with BioMarin's Voxzogo, which requires daily dosing. Phase 3 showed statistically significant improvements in annualized height velocity, muscle strength, and lower limb bowing, with a safety profile that tracked closely to placebo.

ALEX: The weekly-versus-daily comparison in a pediatric population is a compelling clinical story. The review extension in November is what's worth parsing carefully today.

MAYA: Exactly. The FDA extended the review by three months citing a major amendment related to post-marketing requirements — not a safety signal, not a manufacturing deficiency, not a label dispute. Extensions driven by post-marketing architecture rather than data concerns typically signal the agency is still working out what long-term surveillance obligations should look like for a pediatric population with a condition this rare. The label that comes out today will tell us what Ascendis committed to — and that registry framework becomes the baseline BioMarin has to respond to when the weekly-versus-daily dosing conversation moves to payers.

ALEX: Second decision today: Dupixent for allergic fungal rhinosinusitis — which would be the first medication specifically indicated for AFRS if approved. Clean evidence base, label expansion for an already dominant franchise.

MAYA: What the AFRS approval would represent is broader than the patient count implies. Each new Dupixent indication isn't just adding diagnoses — it's deepening payer familiarity with the IL-4/IL-13 pathway across ENT simultaneously. The formulary conversations across rhinosinusitis, CRS with nasal polyps, and AFRS don't happen in silos. Regeneron knows that, and the compounding reimbursement story from sequential indications is part of the strategic logic. If both decisions land today, the market reaction on Monday will tell us whether the commercial reads are landing as expected.
