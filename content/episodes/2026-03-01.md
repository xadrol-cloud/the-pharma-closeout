### [WEEK IN REVIEW — TOP STORY]

ALEX: Week in review. The defining story of the week dropped Sunday morning and it colored everything that followed. Novo Nordisk released headline results from REDEFINE 4 — the open-label Phase 3 head-to-head that put CagriSema directly against Lilly's Zepbound. CagriSema hit 23% weight loss at 84 weeks. Zepbound hit 25.5%. Non-inferiority was not met. The secondary real-world analysis extended the gap — 20.2% versus 23.6%. Coming into Sunday, the weight loss narrative for CagriSema was built around the idea that it could stay close to tirzepatide while doing more on cardiovascular endpoints. That argument just got harder to make.

MAYA: The market's response was measured but unambiguous. Novo's Copenhagen shares dropped 16.5% following the announcement — not a panic, but a decisive re-rating. And that drop didn't happen in isolation. Novo is already down roughly 60% from its peak, so the marginal investor walking into this week was not optimistic to begin with. Seven separate analyst teams published downgrades or model revisions by Friday. Deutsche Bank cut to Hold and took their target from 400 Danish krone to 275. Morningstar revised their CagriSema peak sales forecast from $13 billion down to $8 billion. Those aren't small moves.

ALEX: What the analyst revisions are actually saying is worth unpacking carefully. The $5 billion haircut on peak sales isn't a call that CagriSema fails — it's a call that CagriSema launches into a market where tirzepatide sets the weight loss benchmark, and the premium pricing argument for a drug that doesn't beat tirzepatide on that benchmark is going to be very hard. The $8 billion estimate is still a blockbuster. But the commercial execution now requires Novo to build a differentiation story that bypasses the number patients and prescribers care most about.

MAYA: This is where I want to slow down and look at the trial design, because it matters for what Novo can and cannot claim going forward. REDEFINE 4 was an open-label trial. Both patients and clinicians knew what they were receiving. That's a significant methodological constraint for any patient-reported outcomes — tolerability, quality of life, treatment satisfaction. Patient-reported data from open-label trials carries lower evidentiary weight at FDA precisely because expectation effects and reporting bias are hard to separate from real signal. When Novo makes label claims based on REDEFINE 4, the FDA will apply that scrutiny. The patient-reported outcomes arguments that would strengthen CagriSema's commercial case have to come from the blinded data — and the blinded data hasn't fully read out yet.

ALEX: Which is exactly why the full REDEFINE program structure still matters. REDEFINE 4 was a voluntary head-to-head that Novo ran early, and the primary hypothesis was cardiovascular differentiation and lean mass preservation — not weight loss parity. Those arguments live in other trials and in data that hasn't been presented yet. REDEFINE 11, the cardiovascular outcomes study, is targeted for early 2027. A higher-dose program is entering trials in the second half of this year. The differentiation story isn't dead. It's just been forced to move from weight loss to outcomes.

MAYA: And that's a harder road commercially. Weight loss is the number that patients, prescribers, and payers all understand and use as the primary selection criterion. Cardiovascular outcome differentiation requires the prescribing physician to look at a composite endpoint over three to five years, weigh that against the in-clinic number on the scale, and make a different choice than they would on the basis of the scale alone. That happens in some patient populations — it happens a lot in high-risk cardiovascular patients. The question for Novo's commercial team is whether that population is large enough to justify the infrastructure of a major obesity launch.

ALEX: The competitive read coming out of this week: if you're running commercial strategy at Lilly, REDEFINE 4 did your work for you. Tirzepatide is now the established weight loss benchmark, set by a head-to-head Novo voluntarily initiated. Zepbound's next twelve months look cleaner from a competitive standpoint than they did on Monday morning.

MAYA: The scenario that gets complicated for Lilly is if the REDEFINE cardiovascular data, when it comes, is unambiguously better than what tirzepatide delivers on cardiovascular endpoints. At that point, some population of high-risk patients may shift to CagriSema even with a lower weight loss number. Novo would love for that to be a large population. The market doesn't think it will be. Hence the $8 billion revised forecast. But that's a hypothesis that gets tested with real outcome data over the next two years, not by REDEFINE 4.

### [WEEK IN REVIEW — DEAL LANDSCAPE]

ALEX: On the deal side — the week had volume and direction. Let me bring Marcus Webb in on the anchor transaction.

MARCUS: Looking at the GSK and 35Pharma deal with a weekend's perspective — the $950 million upfront for HS235 tells a clear story about how the pulmonary hypertension market is being priced right now. This is a Phase 1 asset. No patient has been dosed. GSK is paying a premium based on mechanism alone — activin signaling inhibition — and the mechanism's comp is Merck's Winrevair, which hit $1.4 billion in its first year on market. GSK is not buying a de-risked drug. They are buying a position in a market they believe will be worth $18 billion by 2032 and they did not want to wait for Phase 2 data to move, because at that point the price goes up further. The deal logic is straightforward and it's aggressive.

ALEX: The strategic question this deal raises for everyone else watching the pulmonary hypertension space is what happens to remaining independent activin pathway assets. Merck's Winrevair — sotatercept — is already the established comp. GSK now has HS235 in development. If either of those programs demonstrates that activin inhibition has broader cardiovascular applications beyond pulmonary arterial hypertension and HFpEF, the next company with a clean activin mechanism will find buyers moving fast. The 35Pharma deal set a comp for Phase 1 activin assets that the rest of the BD community will be referencing for the next eighteen months.

MAYA: The China licensing acceleration is worth taking seriously as a structural trend rather than a one-week anomaly. Seventeen deals involving China-originated drug candidates in 2026 already — ahead of last year's record pace at this point in the calendar. Western pharma is licensing those assets to fill pipeline gaps rather than waiting to develop the mechanism internally. A third of industry licensing spend in 2025 went to Chinese biotech partnerships. The question isn't whether this continues — it's whether Western regulators and payers eventually push back on the provenance of assets at approval time.

ALEX: One more deal from the week that will get full treatment Monday morning — Gilead announced the acquisition of Arcellx for up to $7.8 billion on Sunday. The centerpiece is anito-cel, a next-generation CAR-T for relapsed or refractory multiple myeloma with a BLA action date in December. The deal structure — $115 per share upfront plus a five-dollar contingent value right tied to $6 billion in cumulative sales by 2029 — tells you something about how seriously Gilead is thinking about commercial ambition in cell therapy. We'll break it down in full Monday.

MAYA: And on the clinical data front — Lilly published ACHIEVE-3 in The Lancet this week, and the oral GLP-1 market read from that data is significant. Orforglipron at 36 milligrams got 31.4% of type 2 diabetes patients to below 5.7% A1C. Oral semaglutide got 11.7% there. Weight loss advantage was 19.7 pounds versus 11 for semaglutide. Those are decisive numbers on the primary endpoints. The single number that complicates the commercial picture is discontinuation at the high dose: 9.7% for orforglipron versus 4.9% for oral semaglutide. Every formulary negotiation Lilly has next year will involve a payer asking whether real-world persistence looks like the clinical trial.

ALEX: The discontinuation gap matters more in the oral market than it does in injectable GLP-1 precisely because oral dosing flexibility is part of the value proposition. Lilly has submissions planned for later this year. The time between approval and real-world persistence data is where the commercial execution risk lives — and that window is where every competitor in the oral GLP-1 space will be pushing.

### [WEEK IN REVIEW — REGULATORY & CLINICAL]

MAYA: Looking at the week's regulatory outcomes. Both FDA decisions from Friday came in, and they tell a coherent story about where the FDA is operating right now. Leading with the navepegritide decision — YUVIWEL from Ascendis Pharma, approved Friday for achondroplasia in pediatric patients two and older. This is the once-weekly injection against BioMarin's Voxzogo, which requires daily dosing. Approved under Accelerated Approval on the basis of statistically significant improvements in annualized height velocity, muscle strength, and lower limb bowing. The weekly dosing difference in a pediatric rare disease population is clinically and commercially significant — adherence over years is a real challenge for families managing daily subcutaneous injections in children.

ALEX: The business story attached to this approval doesn't get enough attention in the regulatory coverage. With the navepegritide approval, Ascendis received a Rare Pediatric Disease Priority Review Voucher. These vouchers trade on the secondary market at roughly $100 to $150 million. Ascendis can retain it to accelerate a future submission or sell it to a large pharma company that needs to move a priority review forward faster. The voucher is a real asset on Ascendis's balance sheet at the moment of approval — and it changes the financial calculus for rare pediatric disease programs across the industry.

MAYA: The second FDA decision: Dupixent approved for allergic fungal rhinosinusitis on Friday — ninth indication for the franchise, first medication specifically indicated for AFRS. The trial basis was 62 patients. Fifty percent showed greater than 50% improvement in opacification at Week 52 versus 10% on placebo. Compelling effect size on a small N, and FDA leaned into it for a condition with no prior approved therapy.

ALEX: The ninth indication tells you something about how Regeneron and Sanofi think about pathway expansion — each new Dupixent approval builds the prescriber's comfort with IL-4/IL-13 across ENT and pulmonology simultaneously. And zooming out: the week's regulatory pattern is consistent — FDA comfortable moving on strong mechanistic evidence in underserved conditions, PRV economics working as designed, and the confirmatory obligation built in as the backstop. That posture is relevant context for anyone watching the FDA calendar in rare disease for the rest of the year.

### [UNDER THE RADAR]

MAYA: Under the radar this week — a dataset that would have commanded significant attention in a quieter news cycle got almost entirely buried by the CagriSema headlines and the Friday approval news. Pfizer and Astellas published results from EV-304, their Phase 3 study of PADCEV plus Keytruda in muscle-invasive bladder cancer treated before surgery. Enfortumab vedotin combined with pembrolizumab delivered a 47% reduction in event-free survival events compared to standard cisplatin-based chemotherapy. Pathological complete response hit 55.8% against 32.5% for chemotherapy. And overall survival showed a 35% reduced risk of death. The primary endpoint was met — and the OS signal on top of it makes this more than a surrogate story.

ALEX: Muscle-invasive bladder cancer is a disease where treatment options have been limited and outcomes have been stubbornly poor relative to other solid tumors. A 47% reduction in events in the pre-surgical setting, backed by an OS benefit, is a meaningful advance — and it extends the PADCEV-Keytruda combination into an earlier treatment line than where it currently operates. ADC-checkpoint combinations displacing platinum-based chemotherapy in the curative setting — not just metastatic disease — is a proof-of-concept that extends well beyond bladder cancer. Every solid tumor program running an ADC-IO perioperative trial just got a reference point for what strong data looks like.

MAYA: The reason this got buried is obvious — REDEFINE 4 dropped the same week in an area the financial media covers with fifteen times the column inches of bladder cancer. But for anyone building pipeline assessments in oncology or tracking the Pfizer-Astellas picture, this is genuinely important data. Watch for a supplemental filing. Any week with a major head-to-head and a pair of FDA approvals is going to create a shadow over solid tumor data — this one deserves a second look.

### [THE WEEK AHEAD]

ALEX: The week ahead. Monday morning is the first market test of how YUVIWEL's approval is being priced into Ascendis. Rare pediatric disease approvals can move adjacent rare disease programs in the sector, and the PRV announcement adds an asset-value dimension to the analyst models. If the secondary PRV market has tightened since the last voucher traded, Monday could give us a quick read on what investors think this one is worth.

MAYA: On Novo Nordisk — this week is critical for narrative management. The leadership team needs to establish the differentiation argument for CagriSema in terms that analysts and investors can incorporate into their models. The cardiovascular outcomes story, the lean mass preservation angle, the higher-dose program timeline — Novo has to get those anchor points into the conversation before the analyst consensus hardens around the revised $8 billion peak sales number. Watch for investor calls, conference presentations, and any Novo-initiated communications about the broader REDEFINE program. What they emphasize about their path to differentiation will set the frame that every subsequent trial readout is judged against.

ALEX: Two FDA action dates to mark this month. March 20th — Rhythm Pharmaceuticals has the Imcivree label expansion decision, setmelanotide for additional MC4R pathway mutations. Clean, narrow regulatory story, but it expands the eligible patient population for an already-approved drug. March 28th is the one to watch more closely: Rocket Pharmaceuticals and their kresladi gene therapy for LAD-1 — leukocyte adhesion deficiency type one, an ultra-rare pediatric immune disorder. If approved, it likely comes with another PRV. Given navepegritide just received a voucher this week, the secondary market will be tracking whether two vouchers from rare pediatric disease hit at the same time and what that does to the price.

MAYA: And looking slightly further out — Novartis has pelacarsen, their lipoprotein(a)-lowering therapy, with Phase 3 cardiovascular outcome data expected in the first half of this year. Lp(a) is one of the most widely recognized cardiovascular risk factors with no approved therapy. If the outcome data is positive, this is one of the more significant cardiovascular readouts in a decade. March is going to be an active month.
