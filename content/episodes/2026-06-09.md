### [TOP STORY — GSK Acquires Nuvalent for $10.6B]

ALEX: GSK confirmed this morning it will acquire Nuvalent for ten point six billion dollars — one hundred twenty-four dollars a share, all cash. Bloomberg and the FT initially reported talks in the nine-to-ten billion range overnight, so the final price came in above expectations. That's a forty percent premium to Nuvalent's last close, twenty-six percent above the thirty-day VWAP. Nuvalent shares surged roughly thirty-nine percent on the news. Net of cash acquired, GSK's aggregate outlay is nine point four billion.

MAYA: The strategic driver is transparent — GSK's best-selling HIV franchise is heading toward patent expiry, and they need to rebuild the revenue base. Nuvalent brings two near-term oncology assets: zidesamtinib, a ROS1-selective inhibitor with a PDUFA date of September eighteenth, and neladalkib, an ALK-selective inhibitor. Both carry breakthrough therapy designation, and both are designed for brain penetrance — which matters enormously in NSCLC, where CNS metastases remain a major driver of morbidity and mortality.

ALEX: Let's bring in Marcus Webb on this one. This is GSK's third company acquisition since late January and the sector's second-largest deal of twenty twenty-six.

MARCUS: Third deal in under five months, each one escalating. What GSK is buying isn't just pipeline — it's near-term commercial optionality with two NDA-stage assets in well-defined lung cancer populations. If both convert, GSK has a franchise. If one stumbles, the other may still justify the premium. The risk premium here reflects how scarce late-stage oncology assets with clean regulatory paths have become.

MAYA: On zidesamtinib specifically — it was engineered to maintain potency against resistance mutations like G2032R while sparing TRK, which is where the tolerability advantage over earlier ROS1 inhibitors originates. If the label reflects that selectivity profile, uptake in the TKI-pretreated population could be rapid.

ALEX: The broader M&A context reinforces the urgency. Average deal value in twenty twenty-six has spiked to five hundred twenty-seven million, up from three hundred sixty-five million last year, according to PitchBook data. The billion-dollar-plus acquisition is becoming table stakes for companies trying to fill pipeline gaps ahead of patent cliffs.

MAYA: I'd push back on calling this a bolt-on, though. GSK's oncology commercial infrastructure is historically thinner than its respiratory and HIV businesses. Nuvalent gives them the drugs — but building a lung cancer commercial engine to compete against Merck, Lilly, and AstraZeneca requires a different capability set entirely. KOL relationships, treatment algorithm positioning — that's years of work compressed into a deal thesis that assumes flawless execution.

ALEX: Fair point. But the September eighteenth PDUFA on zidesamtinib means GSK could have a launched product within months of closing. That compresses the integration timeline considerably.

MAYA: It also concentrates enormous risk on that single decision. A clean September approval validates the entire deal thesis. A delay changes the math overnight.

### [DEAL & PIPELINE ROUNDUP]

ALEX: GSK dominated the headlines, but the deal landscape ran deeper than one mega-acquisition. Eli Lilly signed a licensing agreement with Sweden's AlzeCure Pharma for Alzstatin ACD680, a small-molecule gamma-secretase modulator for Alzheimer's. The deal structure is notable — a meaningful upfront payment with substantial development and commercial milestones that could add significant value over time. Lilly gets global rights to what is currently a preclinical asset, which tells you how far out their conviction runs.

MAYA: The gamma-secretase graveyard looms large — but modulators aren't inhibitors. They redirect cleavage toward shorter amyloid-beta fragments rather than shutting down Notch signaling, which is what torpedoed the earlier programs. What's strategically revealing is that Lilly already has the dominant amyloid antibody franchise and is now layering in an oral small-molecule hedge. They're not betting on one modality — they're betting on the entire amyloid hypothesis surviving.

ALEX: The deal sheet kept running from there. Incyte acquiring Vega Therapeutics — one point two five billion upfront, potentially two billion total — for a late-stage bleeding disorder drug, diversifying beyond Jakafi. J&J picking up Firefly Bio for one billion — Nobel laureate Carolyn Bertozzi's startup merging protein degraders with ADC technology, focused on KRAS. And Roche staking seven hundred million upfront on Nurix Pharmaceuticals' protein-degrading BTK drug, in a deal worth up to two point three billion.

MAYA: When J&J and Roche both independently invest in degrader platforms in the same week, that's not coincidence — it's a thesis going consensus. Targeted protein degradation just graduated from speculative modality to validated M&A category. The read-through for smaller degrader biotechs is that their negotiating leverage just shifted decisively.

ALEX: On the clinical side — a tough day for Gilead. They and Merck ended the EVOKE-03 trial after Trodelvy combined with Keytruda missed its progression-free survival endpoint in first-line NSCLC. That PFS miss surprised analysts at Jefferies and Leerink.

MAYA: This is Trodelvy's second lung cancer setback after the second-line failure, on top of the bladder cancer withdrawal in recent years. The commercial story now narrows to triple-negative breast cancer — a real franchise, but one that raises increasingly pointed questions about whether Gilead will ever recoup its substantial investment in Immunomedics.

ALEX: The offsetting news from the same partnership — Gilead and Merck's islatravir-lenacapavir once-weekly oral combination hit primary endpoints in two Phase 3 trials, proving non-inferior to standard daily regimens. Per the companies' announcement, it has the potential to be the first approved long-acting oral HIV treatment taken once weekly. One partnership, two very different outcomes in the same twenty-four hours.

MAYA: Worth flagging separately — Tango Therapeutics saw its stock surge after pancreatic cancer combination data exceeded expectations in a small trial. Early-stage, but pancreatic cancer has been an innovation desert for decades. When a tumor type that resistant starts generating data that moves markets, the investment thesis in that space is shifting.

ALEX: Also in the financing column, Parabilis Medicines set IPO terms at up to four hundred seventy-five million dollars — that would make it twenty twenty-six's second-largest new biotech stock offering. The IPO window remains wide open.

### [REGULATORY WATCH]

MAYA: Also worth noting on the regulatory side — a clean win to start. FDA expanded Pfizer's Hympavzi to include pediatric hemophilia patients ages six to eleven, as well as patients twelve and older with inhibitors. Per Pfizer's announcement, Hympavzi is now the first subcutaneous non-factor therapy available for pediatric hemophilia B patients in that age group — a genuine expansion of access for families managing treatment burden in young children.

ALEX: The larger regulatory development is geopolitical. WuXi AppTec landed on the Pentagon's 1260H list of companies linked to the Chinese military. WuXi is calling the designation, quote, factually incorrect, saying it has no military affiliation. But the legal mechanism is what counts — 1260H inclusion triggers automatic designation as a biotechnology company of concern under the Biosecure Act. And in Congress, a separate House bill aims to add biotechnology to the COINS Act, following deals involving Pfizer and Bristol Myers with Chinese entities.

MAYA: The pressure is now converging from three directions simultaneously — the Pentagon, Congress, and the Biosecure Act's automatic trigger. For any Western pharma company still heavily reliant on China-based CDMO services, supply chain diversification just moved from strategic option to operational imperative. The companies most exposed aren't the ones without a backup plan — they're the ones still banking on the political winds reversing.
