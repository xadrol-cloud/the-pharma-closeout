### [TOP STORY — AbbVie Decnupaz FDA Approval]

ALEX: Top story. The FDA approved AbbVie's Decnupaz today — pivekimab sunirine-pvzy, a CD123-directed antibody and alkylating agent conjugate for adults with BPDCN. Another novel drug approval logged by the FDA this year, continuing the steady stream of new medicines reaching the market. Breakthrough therapy designation from September twenty-twenty-five, priority review, and approval landed this morning. ABBV closed up about one percent.

MAYA: What's worth unpacking is the payload chemistry. Elzonris delivers a diphtheria toxin fusion — Decnupaz pairs an alkylating agent with the same CD123-targeting antibody backbone. Different cytotoxic strategies against the same surface antigen, and that double validation is the more interesting signal for the field. Clinicians now have a mechanistically distinct alternative in a disease where the toolkit was essentially one deep. And CD123 overexpression on AML blasts and leukemic stem cells means the platform story extends well beyond this one ultra-rare indication.

ALEX: Commercially, though, this is a footnote. BPDCN affects roughly a thousand to fourteen hundred patients a year in the U.S. This is not moving AbbVie's revenue needle.

MAYA: A footnote for the P&L — not for the patients in the clinic. But the Street is not pricing AbbVie on ultra-rare hematology, and everyone in the room knows that.

ALEX: The value here is execution signal. Breakthrough to approval at this pace shows AbbVie's oncology regulatory machine functioning at a high level. That's a process story more than a product story — and it matters when you're evaluating whatever the next program off that CD123 platform turns out to be.

### [DEAL & PIPELINE ROUNDUP]

ALEX: Now — if AbbVie's approval was a small-market story, this is the opposite end of the spectrum. Apogee Therapeutics landed a royalty financing package from Blackstone Life Sciences worth up to one-point-three billion dollars to advance zumilokibart into Phase 3 for atopic dermatitis. Endpoints framed the committed royalty portion at eight hundred million. Either way, substantial non-dilutive capital for a program that hasn't started pivotal trials yet.

MAYA: And this is the readout we've been tracking. Apogee released new Phase 2 data alongside the deal — the Part B dose-optimization results we flagged back in March. Fresh efficacy data paired with a financing announcement at this scale — Blackstone is signaling conviction that zumilokibart has a credible path to competing against Dupixent's thirteen-billion-dollar-plus franchise.

ALEX: Let's bring in Marcus Webb for the deal architecture.

MARCUS: The structure matters more than the headline number. A royalty deal of this magnitude before Phase 3 starts means Blackstone is pricing in a high probability of regulatory success and a commercial profile worth surrendering future revenue share for. Apogee avoids dilution, keeps operational control, and funds the entire pivotal program without a secondary offering weighing on the stock. If this template works, expect every mid-cap biotech sitting on a clean Phase 2 readout to be exploring royalty financing structures before the end of the year.

ALEX: On the pipeline side, Kailera Therapeutics and partner Hengrui posted Phase 1 data for their triple agonist targeting GLP-1, GIP, and glucagon receptors. BioPharma Dive described the early results as suggesting the drug might spur greater weight loss than existing dual agonists. The bar is already public — Lilly's retatrutide delivered twenty-eight-point-three percent weight loss on the twelve-milligram dose at eighty weeks in TRIUMPH-1, with forty-five percent of participants achieving thirty percent or more weight loss. And Kailera's broader obesity portfolio is filling out — their Q1 update highlighted oral ribupatide, a GLP-1/GIP dual agonist, delivering up to twelve-point-one percent weight loss at week twenty-six with no plateau observed.

MAYA: Phase 1 to pivotal is years of attrition, and the dropout rate in this class is real. But Kailera is assembling optionality — a triple injectable and a dual-agonist oral, both Hengrui-partnered. And that partnership ties directly to the COINS Act tension we covered yesterday — Chinese-partnered obesity assets lined up against U.S. incumbents is precisely the scenario that proposed legislation is designed to address.

ALEX: Two quick hits to round out the deal side. CordenPharma is acquiring AmbioPharm, a U.S.-headquartered peptide API contract manufacturer with operations in the U.S. and China. Terms undisclosed. Peptide manufacturing capacity is the critical bottleneck as the GLP-1 market scales, and the CDMOs locking in that supply now hold meaningful leverage when the next wave of oral formulations needs production partners. Separately, J&J dismissed fifty-six employees from its New Jersey headquarters as part of the ongoing orthopedics business separation — small number, but another confirmation that carve-out is proceeding on its announced timeline.

### [REGULATORY WATCH]

MAYA: On the regulatory front — a significant delay. The FDA extended its review of AstraZeneca's camizestrant, the oral SERD filed for first-line ER-positive, HER2-negative advanced breast cancer in patients with emergent ESR1 mutations. This follows the April ODAC meeting where the advisory panel found the SERENA-6 evidence inconclusive.

ALEX: And the competitive timing is brutal. Pfizer and Arvinas landed Veppanu's approval on May first for ESR1-mutant breast cancer. Every week camizestrant sits in extended review, Veppanu deepens its prescriber relationships and builds real-world clinical experience.

MAYA: Here's the budget on what happened. SERENA-6 asked the FDA to endorse a preemptive treatment switch triggered by an emergent ESR1 mutation detected before clinical progression — essentially, approve changing therapy based on a molecular signal rather than observable disease worsening. That's a paradigm the adcomm was not prepared to validate. AstraZeneca filed additional analyses after the negative vote, which triggered this review extension, but overcoming a hostile panel recommendation and a novel regulatory question at the same time — that evidentiary bar just got materially steeper.

ALEX: Also worth noting on the regulatory side — Outlook Therapeutics won an FDA appeal after three complete response letters for LYTENAVA, their bevacizumab ophthalmic formulation for wet AMD. The agency concluded that substantial evidence of effectiveness has been established based on NORSE TWO plus confirmatory data. Outlook is now targeting a Class 1 BLA resubmission in June with a standard review clock. Three rejections, a formal dispute resolution, and now a clear regulatory path — that kind of persistence only pays off if the resubmission is airtight.

MAYA: And one more thread connecting this week's stories. Viridian Therapeutics signed a manufacturing deal with WuXi Biologics for its thyroid eye disease candidate — a direct Tepezza challenger heading toward an FDA decision. A company engaging a Chinese CDMO while BIOSECURE Act pressure intensifies and the COINS Act expansion debate is live. They've either concluded the legislation stalls, or they've calculated near-term manufacturing speed outweighs transition risk later. Neither answer gives policymakers much comfort — and it signals the industry isn't waiting for Washington to make up its mind.
