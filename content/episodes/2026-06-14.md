### [WEEK IN REVIEW — TOP STORY: Lilly's Type II JAK2 Inhibitor at EHA]

ALEX: Week in review — and the defining data package dropped Friday at EHA in Stockholm. Lilly unveiled Phase 1 results from the AJX-101 study — AJ1-11095, the type II selective JAK2 inhibitor acquired through the up-to-two-point-three-billion-dollar Ajax Therapeutics deal. Ajax was cofounded by Ross Levine at Memorial Sloan Kettering, and Lilly had been investing in the company for years before committing to the full buyout. Here's the readout. May twenty-eighth data cutoff. Twenty-three patients, five dose levels from twenty-five to one-hundred-twenty-five milligrams once daily. Every single patient had previously failed a type I JAK2 inhibitor. Median two prior therapies. Best response SVR35 — seventy percent. Sixteen of twenty-three. TSS50 at week twelve — also seventy percent. At week twenty-four, sixty-five percent.

MAYA: Put that seventy percent against the benchmark. Post-JAK inhibitor SVR35 rates in this population have historically ranged from zero to thirty-two percent — that's per the EHA presentation from John Mascarenhas at Mount Sinai. And Lilly is reporting this from a dose-escalation cohort, not a selected expansion group dosed at the sweet spot. That distinction is critical — dose-escalation cohorts are inherently noisy. You have patients at subtherapeutic doses dragging the average down. The fact that the aggregate number still more than doubles the historical ceiling tells you the signal-to-noise ratio here is unusually strong.

ALEX: And the VAF data adds another dimension entirely. Twenty-one of twenty-three patients showed reductions in driver mutation variant allele frequency. Among the seventeen who reached week twenty-four, fifty-nine percent had at least a twenty percent VAF reduction, and thirty-five percent hit fifty percent or greater — including JAK2 mutations specifically.

MAYA: That allelic engagement is the mechanistic signature to watch. Type I inhibitors suppress the downstream cytokine cascade — they don't meaningfully shrink the malignant clone. If VAF reductions at this magnitude hold with longer exposure, the clinical conversation shifts from palliation to disease modification. And I want to underscore why that matters so much commercially — not just scientifically. The entire value proposition of the type I class has been symptom management and spleen reduction. If Lilly can credibly demonstrate clone reduction in a registrational setting, the labeling language alone could redefine how payers tier this therapy relative to existing JAK inhibitors. You're not competing on the same value axis anymore.

ALEX: I want to be disciplined about that framing, though. Twenty-three patients. Phase 1. Dose-escalation. A disease modification claim in myelofibrosis ultimately requires survival data we are years from having. The enthusiasm is warranted by the biology, but the evidentiary bar for that kind of label claim is extraordinarily high, and no one should be modeling it as a near-term certainty.

MAYA: Fair — and I'm not suggesting the claim is earned yet. But the signal is mechanistically distinct from anything the type I class generates. Lilly didn't write a two-point-three-billion-dollar check for incremental symptom relief. Their thesis is disease modification, and nothing in this early dataset contradicts it. That's the minimum threshold for a deal this size. If you're sitting in Lilly's oncology strategy group right now, you're looking at a Phase 1 readout that does exactly what it needed to do — validate the mechanism and justify accelerating into randomized studies. The internal conversation is about speed now, not whether the asset works.

ALEX: Lilly's BD and oncology head Jake Van Naarden told Fierce this medicine "started working right out of the gate." He also made the point that the study was deliberately designed as an entirely JAK2-pretreated population — no frontline-naive patients muddying the efficacy signal. That's a direct challenge to Incyte's Jakafi franchise, and Incyte heard it loud and clear.

MAYA: They heard it from multiple directions. Menarini also presented Phase 3 SENTRY data at the same meeting — selinexor plus ruxolitinib in myelofibrosis. The treatment sequencing conversation in this disease just fractured into something considerably more complex, and Incyte is defending on multiple fronts simultaneously. Think about their strategic position — Jakafi has been the backbone of myelofibrosis therapy for over a decade. Now you have a type II inhibitor demonstrating activity in patients who already failed type I therapy, and a combination approach layering on top of ruxolitinib itself. Both strategies implicitly frame Jakafi as necessary but insufficient. That's a very different competitive narrative than Incyte has had to manage before.

### [WEEK IN REVIEW — DEAL LANDSCAPE]

ALEX: That competitive fracturing feeds directly into the deal landscape, because the companies reshaping these markets are doing it through acquisitions. The marquee number this week: GSK. Ten-point-six billion dollars for Nuvalent — GSK's third buyout since late January, and per BioPharma Dive, the sector's second-largest deal of twenty twenty-six. Endpoints reported that GSK timed the purchase to exploit an ASCO-related dip in Nuvalent's stock. Let's bring in Marcus Webb on that deal logic.

MARCUS: Buying the ASCO dip is textbook opportunism — same pipeline, lower premium. But three acquisitions in five months creates a different kind of risk. Each asset looks sound in isolation. The question now is integration capacity. Execution risk has shifted from pipeline to organization.

ALEX: Marcus puts his finger on the tension. When you're running three parallel integrations, the constraint isn't capital — it's management bandwidth. Every integration team, every commercial launch plan, every regulatory strategy competes for the same senior leadership attention. The strategic logic of each deal can be impeccable, and the portfolio can still underperform if the organizational scaffolding buckles under the weight. That's the risk the market will price in over the coming quarters.

ALEX: On the capital formation side — the IPO window keeps widening. Parabilis Medicines priced at six hundred seventy million — the largest biotech IPO on record, per BioPharma Dive and Endpoints. Days later, cardiovascular biotech Kardigan set terms for up to three-hundred-seventy-three million, targeting a one-point-four-billion-dollar valuation. Lead asset: danicamtiv for genetic dilated cardiomyopathy. If it prices, Kardigan becomes the thirteenth biotech IPO of twenty twenty-six.

MAYA: Flip that optimism, though. Summit Therapeutics pulled a five-hundred-million-dollar share offering just one day after announcing it — citing market conditions. This is a company with a leading PD-1/VEGF cancer program. If they can't hold investor conviction for twenty-four hours, the window is open by invitation only.

ALEX: Exactly right. Appetite for differentiated biotech narratives is clearly strong — Parabilis and Kardigan each bring clean clinical stories and experienced leadership. But the selectivity is intensifying fast, and the gap between the companies that clear the bar and the ones that don't is widening in real time. What we're seeing isn't a broad reopening of the biotech capital markets — it's a bifurcation. The top-tier stories attract oversubscribed books. Everyone else gets Summit's experience. And that bifurcation has downstream consequences for the deal market too, because the companies that can't access public capital become acquisition candidates at compressed valuations. The acquirers with balance sheet firepower — Lilly, GSK, J&J — benefit on both sides of that dynamic.

### [WEEK IN REVIEW — REGULATORY & CLINICAL]

ALEX: The deal activity is one lens on how this market is moving. The clinical data at EHA is the other — and Maya, the J&J plenary may have been the most consequential presentation of the week for practicing hematologists.

MAYA: MonumenTAL-3. Phase 3. Talvey plus Darzalex Faspro, with or without pomalidomide, in earlier-line relapsed or refractory myeloma — patients with at least one prior therapy. PFS rate up to eighty-one-point-three percent versus fifty-one-point-two for standard of care at twenty-four months. Overall survival up to eighty-nine-point-two versus seventy-nine-point-one. Risk reduction: up to seventy-two percent for progression or death, and up to fifty-three percent for death alone. Published simultaneously in the New England Journal.

ALEX: Per J&J's announcement, MonumenTAL-3 is the first Phase 3 study to show superior PFS with a GPRC5D bispecific combination in earlier-line myeloma. A thirty-point PFS gap — that reshapes sequencing discussions for every community oncologist treating relapsed disease. And the simultaneous New England Journal publication signals how the field is receiving this — the peer review process validated the dataset in parallel with the conference presentation. That's the kind of synchronized rollout you see when a sponsor and the academic community both recognize the data as practice-changing.

MAYA: Here's the nuance worth pulling apart. The doublet alone — Talvey plus Darzalex Faspro without pomalidomide — delivered seventy-seven-point-six percent PFS. Adding pomalidomide nudged that to eighty-one-point-three. The incremental lift from the triplet is real but narrow, and that opens room for clinicians to ask which patients truly need the third agent and which are adequately served by the backbone. From a payer perspective, that three-and-a-half-point difference will be scrutinized intensely. If the doublet delivers nearly the same outcome, the cost-effectiveness argument for the triplet becomes much harder to sustain — and that gives formulary committees real leverage in negotiations with J&J.

ALEX: J&J also brought first pivotal data for Imaavy in warm autoimmune hemolytic anemia at the same meeting — extending that FcRn blocker into a new indication. Thirty-plus abstracts in total from J&J at EHA. The myeloma and hematology franchise dominance is hard to argue with.

MAYA: Meanwhile, AbbVie presented nine-year final analysis from CLL14. Venetoclax plus obinutuzumab in first-line CLL. Median PFS: six-point-four years versus three-point-two for the comparator. And the number that lands hardest — median time to next treatment of seven-point-six years after one year of fixed-duration therapy.

ALEX: Seven-point-six years between treatments. That durability data keeps widening the moat around venetoclax-based approaches in first-line CLL. Think about what that means for competitive positioning — any new entrant in first-line CLL now has to demonstrate not just non-inferiority at two or three years, but comparable long-term treatment-free intervals. The bar AbbVie has set with nine years of follow-up is almost impossible to match without a similarly mature dataset, and that maturity takes time no competitor can shortcut.

MAYA: And the CLL landscape compressed further this week. Lilly presented Phase 3 BRUIN CLL-322 results — six-hundred-thirty-nine patients, nearly eighty percent BTK-pretreated. Jaypirca added to venetoclax and rituximab cut progression or death risk by forty-five percent. Median PFS not reached in the Jaypirca arm versus thirty-nine-point-seven months for the doublet. Between AbbVie's durability data and Lilly's intensification data, the CLL treatment paradigm at EHA moved meaningfully in one week. What's strategically interesting is that these two datasets are complementary rather than directly competitive — AbbVie is demonstrating that fixed-duration venetoclax works for years in the frontline, and Lilly is showing that when patients eventually progress through BTK-directed therapy, layering Jaypirca onto a venetoclax backbone recaptures durable responses. The treatment algorithm is becoming more defined, and both companies have data supporting their position within it.

ALEX: Outside hematology — two divergent regulatory outcomes. The FDA approved Merck's Welireg-Keytruda combination as adjuvant treatment for kidney cancer, which expands the HIF-2alpha plus checkpoint footprint into a setting where Merck had no combination positioning before. On the other end, Jazz Pharma's confirmatory Phase 3 for Zepzelca in lung cancer failed. When a confirmatory study doesn't deliver, the downstream implications extend beyond one company — every sponsor holding an accelerated approval with a pending confirmatory readout is recalculating risk tonight. The Zepzelca failure is a reminder that accelerated approval is a regulatory mechanism, not a clinical guarantee, and the conversion rate from accelerated to full approval remains a systemic vulnerability for any company whose revenue base depends on it.

### [UNDER THE RADAR]

MAYA: Under the radar this week — and genuinely underreported — Camurus received a complete response letter from the FDA for Oclaiz, its once-monthly octreotide formulation for acromegaly. This is the second CRL for CAM2029, per the company's announcement on June tenth.

ALEX: A second CRL for a drug with a straightforward clinical rationale. Acromegaly patients on chronic somatostatin analogs need a convenient subcutaneous option — the unmet need is clear, the mechanism is well understood. When the FDA rejects the same application twice, the question shifts from "can they resolve this?" to "at what point do the program economics make it irrational to keep trying?" The development costs compound with each resubmission cycle, the management distraction is significant, and meanwhile the commercial window is narrowing. At some point, the sunk cost argument — "we've invested too much to walk away" — has to yield to a cold-eyed assessment of the remaining return on investment.

MAYA: And the competitive clock doesn't pause. Every month Oclaiz stalls, alternative long-acting somatostatin formulations have an open runway. In a space where dosing convenience is the primary differentiator, time to market may end up mattering more than the efficacy package itself. This is a case where the regulatory setback doesn't just delay revenue — it potentially forecloses the commercial opportunity entirely if a competitor establishes formulary positioning first.

### [THE WEEK AHEAD]

ALEX: The week ahead brings a notable PDUFA date. June twentieth — the FDA decides on cytisinicline for nicotine dependence. The NDA is supported by the ORCA-2 and ORCA-3 trials, both showing significant improvements in smoking abstinence over placebo. If approved, cytisinicline would be the first new pharmacotherapy for smoking cessation since varenicline — representing a substantial gap in treatment innovation.

MAYA: Two decades without a novel option in a disease that kills more Americans than any single cancer. The clinical case was established in the trials — the unresolved question is commercial. How does cytisinicline position on formulary against generic varenicline? Payer access will determine whether this becomes a meaningful franchise or a specialty niche. And it's worth noting the broader strategic context — smoking cessation is a market where efficacy alone has never been sufficient to drive commercial success. Adherence, coverage, and out-of-pocket cost have historically been the binding constraints on uptake. A novel mechanism doesn't automatically solve any of those.

ALEX: Later in the month, June twenty-seventh — Sobi's nanoencapsulated sirolimus plus pegadricase for uncontrolled gout. Different world from this week's hematology focus, but a significant gap in specialty rheumatology.

MAYA: And more immediately — expect the EHA data to filter into analyst models and competitive intelligence decks over the next several days. The Lilly JAK2 readout and J&J's MonumenTAL-3 numbers will be stress-tested by the Street. The market implications crystallize in the follow-through, not the presentation slide.
