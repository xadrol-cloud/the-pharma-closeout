### [TOP STORY — Gilead Hepcludex: First-Ever FDA-Approved HDV Treatment]

ALEX: Gilead just got the FDA to do something that seemed unlikely four years ago. The agency granted accelerated approval to Hepcludex — bulevirtide — making it, per Gilead's press release, the first and only FDA-approved treatment for chronic hepatitis D. Stock closed up two point nine six percent, market cap roughly one hundred sixty-two billion.

MAYA: HDV is the orphan's orphan in viral hepatitis. It requires hepatitis B co-infection to replicate, but drives the most aggressive disease course — faster progression to cirrhosis, significantly elevated hepatocellular carcinoma risk. Global prevalence estimates swing wildly, from twelve to seventy-two million, largely because diagnostic testing has been so inconsistent. Until today, US clinicians had zero approved options.

ALEX: The backstory adds weight here. The FDA rejected bulevirtide roughly four years ago. Gilead brought it back through accelerated approval, and crucially, Hepcludex has been available in Europe since twenty-twenty — giving regulators years of real-world safety and effectiveness data to inform this decision.

MAYA: The European experience extends well beyond safety. Hepatologists there have spent years iterating on treatment duration and combination strategies that US physicians can draw from immediately. But the real commercial unlock is reimbursement — an FDA indication opens payer coverage, and Gilead brings hepatology field infrastructure that no smaller developer could replicate.

ALEX: At the portfolio level, this deepens Gilead's diversification beyond HIV and HCV. Hepcludex, the expanding oncology book, the Arcellx cell therapy acquisition — the investment thesis is broadening quarter by quarter.

MAYA: Careful with that narrative on day one. Accelerated approval means a confirmatory study is still required, and in HDV that trial design is genuinely difficult — slow natural history, limited patient numbers, and a real question about whether virologic response durably predicts histologic benefit. That post-marketing commitment could stretch for years.

ALEX: You're right that the confirmatory timeline is underappreciated — it's tail risk the market discounts too quickly. But the commercial clock starts regardless, and in a disease where clinicians have literally never had a drug to prescribe, how fast Gilead builds screening awareness and physician engagement is the near-term catalyst to watch.

### [DEAL & PIPELINE ROUNDUP]

ALEX: Gilead wasn't even the biggest story to move money today. On the deal and pipeline side, ASCO twenty twenty-six abstracts dropped Thursday and the market response Friday was emphatic. Merck surged five point six four percent to close at one twenty-two forty-one. On a two hundred seventy-nine billion dollar market cap, that translates to roughly fifteen billion in value creation in a single session — the kind of move that signals real confidence in the post-Keytruda pipeline. Lilly climbed two point two four percent to one thousand sixty-five. And the sector split was revealing: drug manufacturers up one point seven seven percent as a group, biotech down half a percent. Large-cap pharma with late-stage oncology data is where the ASCO money flowed.

MAYA: On the micro-cap end of that spectrum, Akari Therapeutics surged ninety-six percent on about six hundred thousand shares after their ASCO abstract described AKTX-101 showing synergistic activity in KRAS-mutated pancreatic cancer models — with what the company called superior potency versus traditional TROP2 ADCs. Thin float, thin volume. But KRAS-pancreatic is exactly the kind of translational signal that reprices a micro-cap during abstract season.

ALEX: Phase 3 data from Sobi next — the REDUCE 2 study of pozdeutinurad in gout hit its primary endpoint. Sixty-nine point two percent versus eight point one on placebo. Let that delta sit for a moment. Sixty-one percentage points of separation.

MAYA: You almost never see treatment effects that size in a chronic metabolic disease study. For a next-generation oral URAT1 inhibitor, it simplifies the regulatory conversation considerably. But gout's commercial problem has never been the pharmacology — it's been adherence and systematic underdiagnosis. Whether pozdeutinurad cracks that behavioral wall is what determines actual peak sales, not the response rate.

ALEX: The miss of the day — Biogen and Denali are discontinuing BIIB122 in idiopathic Parkinson's disease after the Phase 2b LUMA study fell short. We've previously framed Denali as a pipeline to watch as their programs read out — and this is a real setback. The drug was well tolerated, per the companies, but the efficacy signal wasn't there.

MAYA: The distinction that matters going forward — Denali independently continues BEACON, a Phase 2a in carriers of a pathogenic LRRK2 variant. Idiopathic Parkinson's is pathologically heterogeneous in ways that may simply overwhelm a single-target kinase approach. If BEACON delivers signal in that genetically defined cohort, the LRRK2 thesis survives — but in a far narrower commercial frame than anyone was modeling two years ago.

ALEX: Also worth noting — Parabilis filed IPO plans after raising eight hundred million privately and signing a one hundred twenty-five million dollar Regeneron deal last week. That would make them the fifth biotech this year to go public after raising at least seven hundred million in private funding. The window isn't just open — it's pulling in companies that could comfortably stay private but see a public market premium they don't want to leave on the table.

ALEX: And on the workforce front, AbbVie disclosed eighty-five layoffs at its Allergan site in Irvine, California, effective July twentieth, per a WARN notice. Novartis is also trimming US headcount, specifics undisclosed. Two top-ten pharma companies restructuring on the same Friday — worth watching whether this is routine portfolio management or early belt-tightening heading into the back half of the year.

### [REGULATORY WATCH]

MAYA: On the regulatory side — and this connects directly to the morning's ADC theme — the day's second FDA approval was Datroway, datopotamab deruxtecan, from AstraZeneca and Daiichi Sankyo. Approved in first-line metastatic triple-negative breast cancer for patients who are not candidates for checkpoint inhibitor therapy.

ALEX: That label carves a direct lane against Gilead's Trodelvy in the TROP2 ADC class. A first-line indication — even in a biomarker-defined subset — gives AZ and Daiichi a sequencing advantage that anchors formulary conversations from here forward.

MAYA: The checkpoint-ineligible population is precisely where first-line systemic options have been thinnest in TNBC — concentrated unmet need. And the CHMP recommended Datroway for EU approval this same week, so the transatlantic regulatory path is running in parallel. That alignment accelerates the next round of indication filings.

ALEX: Two more CHMP positive opinions out of Europe on Friday. Novo Nordisk's oral Wegovy — semaglutide twenty-five milligrams — received the nod for obesity in the EU. On the trial product estimand, mean weight loss reached sixteen point six percent, with one in three patients achieving twenty percent or greater. And Boehringer Ingelheim secured a positive opinion for nerandomilast in IPF and progressive pulmonary fibrosis.

MAYA: The Novo numbers land right in the heart of the oral GLP-1 race. Lilly's Foundayo has been on the US market since April. Now Novo's pill gets the EU green light with strong weight-loss data behind it. Second-half twenty twenty-six is shaping up as the most intensely contested stretch in metabolic disease — and payer formulary positioning may end up mattering more than clinical differentiation between these pills.

ALEX: And a policy story with real teeth. The GOP chairman of the House China select committee is pushing for a biotech clampdown and specifically named Bristol Myers Squibb's fifteen point two billion dollar Hengrui deal from May twelfth, per Endpoints News. More than a dozen early-stage drugs now sitting in the BIOSECURE Act's crosshairs.

MAYA: If this gains legislative traction, it threatens the entire China-to-US licensing pipeline — not just BMS. Multiple large-cap companies have built mid-stage programs around Chinese-originated assets. That's the single biggest strategic overhang heading into summer for any BD team working this corridor.
