### [WEEK IN REVIEW — TOP STORY: ASCO 2026 Preview]

ALEX: The week in review starts with what's coming. ASCO twenty-twenty-six kicks off Thursday, May twenty-ninth in Chicago. Abstracts released on the twenty-second, and this is the fullest preview we've had in years. Five plenary presentations. The bar for plenary selection is as high as it gets in oncology — and the lineup tells you where the field is heading.

MAYA: Two of those five slots belong to Lilly. LIBRETTO-432 reports primary event-free survival for adjuvant Retevmo in RET fusion-positive non-small cell lung cancer — targeted therapy moving into the curative-intent setting. SARC041 is an investigator-initiated Phase 3 of Verzenio in advanced dedifferentiated liposarcoma, where CDK4 amplification is a dominant driver but we've never had randomized Phase 3 evidence to back up the biology. Both could rewrite treatment guidelines.

ALEX: And monarchE overall survival data for Verzenio in early breast cancer is also on the ASCO agenda. A previously published interim analysis showed a favorable OS trend — hazard ratio of zero point nine-oh-three with four hundred forty-two deaths, seven-year OS of eighty-six point eight percent with Verzenio versus eighty-five percent with endocrine therapy alone. If the updated cut sharpens that separation, it cements the adjuvant story.

MAYA: Beyond Lilly, three more plenary trials. Johnson and Johnson's PROTEUS — perioperative apalutamide in high-risk prostate cancer — is being positioned as a potential shift in curative-intent surgical treatment. Revolution Medicines with RASolute 302 — daraxonrasib in previously treated metastatic pancreatic cancer. The company already called their topline results an unprecedented OS benefit. And HARMONi-6, ivonescimab in squamous NSCLC from Akeso and Summit, is a late-breaking plenary abstract scheduled for May thirty-first.

ALEX: Lilly isn't stopping at the plenary, either. They're showing first clinical results from an investigational Nectin-4 ADC in urothelial carcinoma, updated data from Kelonia's in vivo CAR-T program in myeloma. When you step back — small molecule, ADC, cell therapy, targeted therapy — this isn't a single-catalyst week for Lilly. It's a portfolio-wide statement.

MAYA: Breadth is the floor, though. Every big pharma can paper a conference wall with abstracts. The question is whether any of this data rewrites treatment algorithms. Plenary selection means the reviewers think it could — but I've seen plenary presentations generate standing ovations at ASCO and then gather dust on the guidelines shelf.

ALEX: I'll concede that. Plenary doesn't guarantee commercial impact. But the selection bar is high, and when you stack LIBRETTO-432 next to the monarchE update, the first Nectin-4 proof-of-concept, and the Kelonia in vivo CAR-T data, this starts looking like a portfolio inflection for Lilly. The market is going to trade it that way.

MAYA: The broader implication for the whole meeting — ASCO twenty-twenty-six is really a stress test of precision oncology's next chapter. Targeted adjuvant therapy in lung cancer. CDK4 inhibition in sarcoma. Direct RAS inhibition in pancreatic cancer. Bispecific immunotherapy in lung. If even half of these deliver, the solid tumor treatment map looks different by June.

### [WEEK IN REVIEW — REGULATORY & CLINICAL]

MAYA: On the regulatory and clinical side — this week drew a hard line between winners and losers. Start with the loss. Regeneron's fianlimab plus Libtayo did not significantly outperform Keytruda monotherapy in late-stage melanoma. Analysts were caught off guard. Billions in market value evaporated.

ALEX: The Keytruda moat just got wider. Regeneron had built a credible immuno-oncology challenge around that LAG-3 combination, and this result narrows their path substantially.

MAYA: Here's what makes it structurally damaging: melanoma is where LAG-3 inhibition already has regulatory precedent with Opdualag. If you can't beat PD-1 monotherapy in the tumor type most favorable to your mechanism, the development calculus for every other LAG-3 combination study in your pipeline fundamentally changes. Regeneron isn't just absorbing a trial miss — they're reassessing an entire strategic thesis.

ALEX: Now the wins. BioMarin's Phase 3 CANOPY-HCH-3 study of Voxzogo in hypochondroplasia delivered cleanly. Primary endpoint — annualized growth velocity — improved by two point three-three centimeters per year over placebo at week fifty-two, p-value below zero point zero-zero-zero-one. Standing height and height Z-score both hit high significance.

MAYA: I'd pull your attention to the arm span data. Significant at p equals zero point zero-zero-four. That endpoint rarely gets the headline, but arm span is the measure most directly tied to functional independence in skeletal dysplasias — whether a child can reach a countertop, open a door, do the things that determine how they navigate daily life. Regulators notice when you show functional reach alongside growth velocity. BioMarin plans an sNDA filing in the third quarter.

ALEX: Regenxbio also delivered in Duchenne. Pivotal Phase 3 AFFINITY DUCHENNE data for RGX-202 — ninety-three percent of patients hit microdystrophin expression above ten percent at week twelve, n equals thirty, highly statistically significant.

MAYA: The number that earned the accelerated approval conversation is the demonstrated correlation between microdystrophin expression and functional improvement on the NSAA in nine patients. That correlation is Regenxbio's evidentiary bridge — it's what links the surrogate to a clinical outcome FDA cares about. And it positions RGX-202 squarely against Elevidys as the next entrant in DMD gene therapy.

ALEX: Also worth noting on the regulatory front — AstraZeneca and Daiichi Sankyo's Datroway was approved in first-line triple-negative breast cancer on the twenty-second. And the EU CHMP recommended both AstraZeneca's Etcamah and Novo Nordisk's oral Wegovy — that oral semaglutide recommendation is one to watch commercially as it moves toward formal authorization.

MAYA: Then there's the Leqembi Iqlik subcutaneous PDUFA. It was originally today — May twenty-fourth. Pushed to August twenty-fourth after the FDA requested additional information and classified it as a major amendment. The companies say there are no approvability concerns, but a three-month extension means the Alzheimer's commercial conversation pauses while oncology dominates the next month.

ALEX: And Lilly's triple agonist — the GLP-1, GIP, glucagon combination — hit its primary endpoint in Phase 3 for obesity. We've been tracking Lilly's multi-agonist strategy in this space for months.

MAYA: Each positive readout reinforces the same thesis: the next generation of weight management won't come from optimizing a single pathway. It'll come from combining targets. The question is whether the safety profile of triple agonism supports the efficacy gains — and that data will define whether this is a commercial product or a scientific proof of concept.

### [UNDER THE RADAR]

MAYA: Under the radar this week — Immunovant. They reported Phase 2 data for IMVT-1402 in difficult-to-treat rheumatoid arthritis. These are patients who had failed at least two prior advanced therapy mechanisms — the population most companies have walked away from because response rates are historically so low. Week sixteen ACR20: seventy-two point seven percent. ACR50: fifty-four point five percent. ACR70: thirty-five point eight percent. Shares surged nearly thirty-two percent.

ALEX: Those response rates in a multi-mechanism-failure RA population are remarkable. The FcRn inhibitor class has been building momentum in autoimmune, but this is one of the strongest signals we've seen in refractory RA specifically. If the randomized portion confirms these open-label numbers, Immunovant's commercial story expands well beyond the indications the Street has been modeling.

MAYA: The ripple effect is what I'm watching. Difficult-to-treat RA has been an orphaned population inside a common disease — everyone knows these patients exist, but most development programs have avoided them because the bar felt unreachable. If these results replicate, they don't just validate IMVT-1402 — they reopen a therapeutic space the industry had quietly abandoned.
