### [TOP STORY — NOVO'S $506M IRELAND BET ON ORAL WEGOVY]

ALEX: Top story. Novo Nordisk announced a 432 million euro — approximately $506 million U.S. — investment to expand its manufacturing facility in Athlone, Ireland. The expansion is specifically for oral dosage form production: tableting capacity for oral semaglutide. This is the manufacturing infrastructure behind oral Wegovy.

MAYA: Let's put that number in context. Novo is currently under sustained analyst pressure following REDEFINE 4's weight loss result for CagriSema — a result that narrowed the differentiation argument against tirzepatide. Multiple banks have revised their Novo targets downward. And in the middle of that, Novo is writing a half-billion dollar check for oral GLP-1 tableting capacity. That's not a defensive posture. That's a statement about where they believe the demand is going.

ALEX: The underlying case for the investment is the oral Wegovy trajectory. By mid-February, oral Wegovy had reached approximately 246,000 patients on therapy. To understand what that number means, you have to hold it against the injectable Wegovy launch curve from 2021. Oral Wegovy is tracking ahead of where injectable Wegovy was at the same point post-launch — a product that went on to become the commercial anchor of a multi-hundred-billion-dollar franchise. Novo is reading that trajectory and making a manufacturing bet before supply becomes the ceiling on growth.

MAYA: The strategic logic is straightforward once you accept the demand premise. Oral GLP-1 removes the injection barrier, which is real and significant — surveys consistently put needle hesitancy in the 25 to 40 percent range among eligible patients who haven't started therapy. If the conversion from oral-eligible patients to active therapy accelerates, the bottleneck shifts from demand to supply. Novo is trying to ensure supply doesn't become the constraint that costs them market share at the moment of category inflection. The Ireland expansion is insurance against their own success.

ALEX: There's also a competitive dimension to the timing. Lilly's orforglipron — a once-daily small-molecule oral GLP-1 — is moving through FDA review with a Q2 2026 decision expected. Unlike oral semaglutide, which requires specific dosing conditions, orforglipron is a pill with no food or water restrictions. If orforglipron lands this summer with a clean label, Novo needs oral Wegovy to be deeply embedded in prescribing habits before that launch hits. Manufacturing capacity is part of that entrenchment strategy — you can't hold market share you can't supply.

MAYA: The analyst story this morning is the Morgan Stanley upgrade. Morgan Stanley moved Novo Nordisk from Underweight to Equal Weight — which is worth understanding clearly for what it is and what it isn't. Their note cited approximately 56% potential upside at current valuations and described the stock as "difficult to ignore" at its current level. That's a floor call, not a conviction buy. They're saying the downside risk has been adequately priced in following the CagriSema-driven selloff. They're not saying the growth thesis is intact. The divided room remains divided: Barclays maintains a DKK 270 target, down from 340, and JPMorgan downgraded in late February. Morgan Stanley stepping off the Underweight call is a calibration, not a turning point.

ALEX: What you have this morning is Novo sending two signals simultaneously — one to the market through the Morgan Stanley upgrade landing, and one to its own commercial organization through the manufacturing commitment. The message is: we are not retreating from oral GLP-1 as the growth vector. We are doubling down on the infrastructure to compete for the oral market before orforglipron changes the competitive dynamics. Whether that conviction is rewarded depends entirely on how quickly oral GLP-1 penetration accelerates in the next 18 months.

### [SECONDARY STORY — GLP-1 MARKET SHARE MATH]

MAYA: Let's run the market share numbers, because they tell the story of why the manufacturing investment matters. Current injectable GLP-1 market share in obesity: Lilly at 60.5 percent across Zepbound and Mounjaro, Novo at 39.1 percent across Wegovy and Ozempic. That's a roughly 21-point gap, and it has been widening since tirzepatide's approval.

ALEX: That gap is the commercial consequence of REDEFINE 4. The trial didn't fail — CagriSema produced meaningful weight loss at 23 percent over 84 weeks. But the market had priced Novo on the assumption that CagriSema would be definitively superior to tirzepatide on the headline efficacy metric. It wasn't. And in a category where weight loss percentage at 52 or 72 weeks has become the primary formulary decision variable, the differentiation argument collapsed.

MAYA: Oral is where Novo is trying to rebuild differentiation. Not because oral semaglutide out-performs tirzepatide on weight loss — it doesn't, not at current approved doses — but because oral access creates a distinct patient pathway. Patients who won't self-inject can now access GLP-1 therapy at all. That's a different population than the one Lilly is competing for with Zepbound. Novo's commercial framing is shifting from "better than tirzepatide" to "accessible where tirzepatide isn't."

ALEX: Orforglipron complicates that framing. Lilly's oral small molecule doesn't require special dosing conditions — no 30-minute wait before eating, no water volume restriction. If orforglipron launches in Q2 with a clean label and achieves comparable weight loss to oral semaglutide in real-world use, the oral-access argument becomes a two-player race instead of Novo's exclusive lane. The Ireland manufacturing expansion is, in part, a race to build installed prescriber base before that happens. Six months of oral Wegovy in primary care practices is a meaningful defensive moat if orforglipron lands in August with a comparable clinical profile.

### [REGULATORY BRIEF]

MAYA: On the regulatory calendar — BMS has a PDUFA date for Sotyktu, deucravacitinib, on March 6th. That's this Friday. The indication is psoriatic arthritis — a new indication beyond the plaque psoriasis label Sotyktu already holds. The Phase 3 data showed 54.2 percent of patients achieving ACR20 response at week 16 versus 34.1 percent on placebo. That's a statistically significant and clinically meaningful difference on the primary endpoint.

ALEX: The strategic significance here is the mechanism. Deucravacitinib is a TYK2 inhibitor — tyrosine kinase 2. If the FDA approves the PsA indication, Sotyktu becomes the first TYK2 inhibitor approved in psoriatic arthritis. The existing PsA drug landscape is dominated by TNF inhibitors, IL-17 agents, and IL-23 inhibitors — all biologics, all injectable. A well-tolerated oral with a distinct mechanism adds real differentiation in a crowded class, particularly for patients who've failed or are intolerant to existing options.

MAYA: The safety profile of deucravacitinib is the underappreciated competitive asset here. The drug was designed to avoid the pan-JAK inhibition that earned the FDA black box warning on drugs like tofacitinib and baricitinib — warnings that created significant prescribing friction across the JAK class. Deucravacitinib's TYK2 selectivity means it entered the market without those warnings, which in practice means fewer hurdles with payers, prescribers, and risk management programs. If Friday's approval comes without new label restrictions, BMS has a meaningful commercial window in PsA before biosimilar competition hits the IL-17 agents.

ALEX: Two more regulatory dates to flag for the next three weeks. Setmelanotide — Imcivree, from Rhythm Pharmaceuticals — has a PDUFA on March 20th for an expanded indication in MC3R deficiency and hypothalamic obesity. And brensocatib — Kresladi, from Insmed — has a PDUFA on March 28th for bronchiectasis. That's the DPP-1 inhibitor that Insmed has positioned as a potential standard-of-care shift in a disease area that hasn't had an approved targeted therapy. The Kresladi decision is one of the more commercially significant calls of Q1 for a company that has been building toward this moment for years.

### [WHAT TO WATCH]

ALEX: Looking ahead. Friday's Sotyktu PsA decision is the most immediate catalyst on the calendar — three days away. If approved, watch how BMS characterizes the commercial rollout timeline in a market where they're already established with plaque psoriasis prescribers. The rheumatology channel is a different call point than dermatology, and BMS will need a distinct pull-through strategy for PsA.

MAYA: The oral GLP-1 story has a second chapter coming in the next few months. Orforglipron's Q2 FDA decision is the event that will either validate or test Novo's Ireland manufacturing bet. Watch for the label — specifically whether the FDA imposes any dosing guidance that limits orforglipron's convenience advantage over oral semaglutide. A clean label with no food-water restrictions confirmed in the prescribing information would be a materially different competitive signal than a label that adds caveats.

ALEX: And in Alzheimer's disease — Lilly's remternetug, an anti-amyloid antibody currently in Phase 3 in the TRAILRUNNER-ALZ-1 trial, has Phase 3 readout data expected in March. Lilly has been building out its Alzheimer's infrastructure following donanemab's approval, and remternetug is positioned as a potential next-generation asset in the same mechanism class. If the TRAILRUNNER data reads positively, it becomes a pipeline milestone that supports Lilly's Alzheimer's franchise buildout. Given the donanemab FDA journey, watch for how quickly Lilly is prepared to move toward regulatory submission if the data support it.
