### [TOP STORY — MODERNA'S $2.25B LNP PATENT SETTLEMENT]

ALEX: Top story. Moderna has agreed to pay up to $2.25 billion to settle the lipid nanoparticle patent dispute with Genevant Sciences and Arbutus Biopharma — both tied to Roivant Sciences. The deal, announced yesterday, ends one of the most contentious intellectual property battles in biotech history.

MAYA: Let's break down the payment structure, because it tells you something important about the legal strategy. Moderna pays $950 million upfront in July 2026. The remaining $1.3 billion is contingent on the outcome of a legal appeal under 28 U.S.C. Section 1498 — the government contractor immunity statute. Moderna has argued that because its COVID vaccines were produced under a government contract during a public health emergency, patent infringement liability should fall on the government, not the manufacturer. If Moderna wins that appeal, the $1.3 billion is extinguished. If it loses, the full $2.25 billion comes due.

ALEX: The core of this dispute was never about mRNA itself. Moderna's mRNA technology — the sequence design, the modified nucleosides — that was their own. The fight was about the delivery vehicle: lipid nanoparticles. LNPs are the molecular envelopes that carry the mRNA payload into cells. Without functional LNP technology, you don't have a viable mRNA vaccine. Genevant and Arbutus held foundational patents on LNP composition and manufacturing methods. Moderna used that technology in Spikevax, and the question was whether the government contract shielded them from the patent claims.

MAYA: The settlement gives Moderna a global non-exclusive license to LNP technology for infectious disease applications, plus a covenant not to sue on certain Genevant and Arbutus patents. That's the strategic unlock — Moderna now has clean freedom to operate across its infectious disease pipeline, including flu, RSV, and combination vaccines, without an LNP patent cloud hanging over every product. The license scope is important: infectious disease applications. It does not appear to extend to Moderna's oncology or rare disease programs, which use different LNP formulations but could still face IP questions.

ALEX: For the broader mRNA field, this settlement sets a price benchmark. If your mRNA platform depends on licensed LNP technology, you now know what it costs to settle — roughly a billion dollars per blockbuster product cycle. That's a meaningful line item for companies building mRNA pipelines, and it reinforces the strategic value of owning your own delivery technology rather than licensing it. BioNTech, CureVac, and anyone entering the mRNA space should be reading this settlement as a cost-of-goods signal for their own IP strategies.

MAYA: One more dimension worth flagging. Roivant — Vivek Ramaswamy's holding company — stands to collect significantly from this deal through its Genevant stake. The settlement validates Roivant's model of acquiring undervalued biotech IP and monetizing it through litigation or licensing. It's a reminder that in pharma, the most valuable assets aren't always the drugs themselves — sometimes they're the enabling technologies that every drug depends on.

### [SECONDARY STORY — FDA COMPOUNDED GLP-1 ENFORCEMENT]

ALEX: Shifting to the GLP-1 space. The FDA issued 30 warning letters yesterday to telehealth companies over the marketing and sale of compounded GLP-1 receptor agonists. This is the agency's most aggressive enforcement action in the compounding space to date.

MAYA: The violations centered on two categories. First, claims that implied compounded GLP-1 products were equivalent to or interchangeable with FDA-approved products like Wegovy and Ozempic. Compounded drugs are not FDA-approved — the agency does not review their safety, efficacy, or manufacturing quality before they reach patients. Second, branding practices where telehealth firms marketed compounded products under their own company names without disclosing the actual compounder, creating the impression that the telehealth company itself was the manufacturer.

ALEX: The most concerning case involved GenoGenix, where the FDA identified manufacturing quality issues that resulted in three patients being sent to the emergency room after taking a non-GLP-1 compounded medication produced by the same facility. That's the risk regulators have been warning about: when compounding pharmacies operate at commercial scale without FDA manufacturing oversight, quality failures aren't hypothetical — they're patient safety events.

MAYA: FDA Commissioner Marty Makary framed this as the beginning of a broader enforcement campaign, not a one-time action. The statement specifically noted that the agency is monitoring claims across "all media platforms" — which signals that social media marketing of compounded GLP-1s, which has been largely unchecked, is now within enforcement scope. For the branded manufacturers — Novo Nordisk and Eli Lilly in particular — this is a commercial tailwind. Every compounding exit is a potential conversion to branded therapy. Yesterday we talked about Novo's oral Wegovy ramp to 246,000 patients and the Ireland manufacturing expansion. The regulatory pressure on compounders removes a low-cost competitor from the market at exactly the moment Novo is trying to accelerate oral GLP-1 adoption.

ALEX: The timing matters for another reason. Tirzepatide was removed from the FDA drug shortage list in late 2024, and semaglutide followed in early 2025. Once a drug exits the shortage list, the legal basis for compounding narrows significantly under the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act. These warning letters are the enforcement consequence of that regulatory shift. Compounders who built businesses during the shortage window are now operating in a market where the legal justification for their products has eroded, and the FDA is making that point explicitly.

### [REGULATORY BRIEF]

MAYA: On the regulatory calendar — the Sotyktu PDUFA is now two days away. Friday, March 6th, BMS expects the FDA decision on deucravacitinib for psoriatic arthritis. We covered the clinical data yesterday — 54.2% ACR20 at week 16 versus 34.1% placebo — so today the update is on the commercial readiness signal. A Spherix Global Insights survey fielded in January among 104 U.S. rheumatologists found that one in three view deucravacitinib as a substantial advance over currently available PsA therapies. Nearly half were already aware of the upcoming PDUFA decision. That level of pre-approval awareness in a specialty that BMS doesn't traditionally own is a meaningful signal of pull-through potential.

ALEX: Also worth flagging: GSK's linerixibat has a PDUFA date on March 24th for cholestatic pruritus in primary biliary cholangitis. Linerixibat is an IBAT inhibitor — ileal bile acid transporter — and the GLISTEN Phase 3 enrolled 238 PBC patients with moderate to severe itch. PBC pruritus has limited approved options, and the FDA has granted orphan drug designation. If approved, linerixibat would be a meaningful addition to a disease area that hepatologists have been underserved in for years.

MAYA: And BridgeBio announced today that additional data from the Phase 3 FORTIFY trial of BBP-418 in limb-girdle muscular dystrophy type 2I will be presented at the MDA Clinical and Scientific Conference next week in Orlando — March 8th through 11th. The interim analysis met its primary efficacy endpoint with a 1.8-fold increase in glycosylated alpha-dystroglycan at three months. BridgeBio has said it intends to file an NDA in the first half of this year. For a rare neuromuscular disease with no approved targeted therapy, the FORTIFY data represent a potential first-in-class opportunity.
