### [TOP STORY — Lantheus Weighs ~$7B Sale to Curium]

ALEX: Top story. Lantheus Holdings is considering a sale after receiving a takeover offer from private-equity-backed Curium Pharma — valuation approximately seven billion dollars. Bloomberg broke it Friday, Reuters confirmed same day, and Lantheus stock surged seven-point-four percent on the report.

MAYA: What makes this interesting isn't just the number — it's where the bid is aimed. Most radiopharma M&A this cycle has targeted the therapeutic side. Radioligand therapies, targeted alpha emitters. Curium going after Lantheus is a supply-chain play — they want the diagnostic and imaging infrastructure. That changes the conversation about how this ecosystem consolidates.

ALEX: Let's bring in Marcus Webb on this one.

MARCUS: Curium is already one of the largest nuclear medicine companies globally. At seven billion, they're paying for an installed commercial footprint — hospital contracts, imaging center relationships across North America. Not a pipeline bet. The key signal is that Lantheus hasn't accepted. If they open a formal sale process and strategic bidders with therapeutic radiopharma pipelines enter, this price moves up meaningfully.

ALEX: And the strategic logic for a competing bid writes itself. Reuters reported that total biotech M&A deal value reached record levels in Q1 alone — sellers have real leverage right now. A company like Novartis or AstraZeneca that's building out radioligand therapy could see Lantheus's diagnostic platform as the companion infrastructure for their own programs. Curium wants a supply chain. A strategic buyer might want a theranostics backbone.

MAYA: That distinction matters clinically. Pairing diagnostic imaging capability with therapeutic delivery isn't just vertical integration — it's the architecture for patient selection and treatment monitoring that makes radioligand therapy commercially scalable. A PE buyer optimizes margins. A strategic buyer builds a treatment ecosystem. Those are different valuations.

ALEX: However this plays out — standalone deal or competitive auction — it sets the benchmark for how the market prices radiopharma platform assets for the rest of 2026.

### [DEAL & PIPELINE ROUNDUP]

ALEX: Lantheus isn't the only deal reshaping the landscape. Eli Lilly quietly acquired Engage Biologics — a preclinical company pioneering non-viral DNA delivery. No terms disclosed. This is Lilly building genetic medicine infrastructure piece by piece, specifically going after the delivery bottleneck that's held the entire field back.

MAYA: Non-viral delivery is the enabling play to watch in genetic medicine. The immunogenicity ceiling on AAV vectors has been the fundamental constraint on broadening gene therapy beyond one-and-done rare disease applications. If Engage's platform unlocks redosing, chronic disease indications open up — and that's a market-size conversation that's orders of magnitude larger.

ALEX: Meanwhile, BMS's Hengrui partnership is drawing political fire. The GOP chairman of the House China panel is publicly calling out the deal — worth up to fifteen-point-two billion dollars per STAT News for more than a dozen early-stage drugs. We've previously framed this as a make-or-break year for BMS. Geopolitical risk on top of pipeline pressure complicates things considerably.

MAYA: The irony is sharp. BMS went to Hengrui precisely because they needed pipeline replenishment at scale. If the political environment narrows access to China-origin licensing, that doesn't just hit BMS — it shrinks the deal surface for every large pharma company facing patent cliff pressure. The downstream effect is fewer options and higher prices for the assets that remain accessible.

ALEX: Quick numbers on BridgeBio — new acoramidis data presented at Heart Failure 2026, Q1 revenue beat driven by surging Attruby sales. Stock up ninety-seven percent over the past year, market cap around thirteen-point-two billion, and a freshly authorized five-hundred-million-dollar buyback.

MAYA: Confidence signal from management, but Morningstar pegs fair value at forty-eight dollars against a Friday close of sixty-nine twelve — a seventeen percent premium. The market is pricing in aggressive share capture from Pfizer's tafamidis franchise, and that kind of multiple needs quarter-over-quarter commercial validation to sustain. One soft Attruby print resets the narrative fast.

ALEX: On the setback side — two stories converging at Biogen. First, the company and Denali Therapeutics ended development of BIIB122 for idiopathic Parkinson's disease. Phase 2b missed its primary endpoint — the drug did not significantly slow disease progression.

MAYA: We've previously framed Denali's pipeline as one to watch as programs read out. This readout landed, and it's a clear negative. Applying a genetically-informed mechanism to the idiopathic population was always the higher-risk path — the biological heterogeneity in that group makes detecting a disease-modification signal extraordinarily difficult. The denominator is working against you from the start.

ALEX: And that's not the only miss this month. Biogen's Phase 2 CELIA trial for diranersen — their tau-targeting Alzheimer's program — failed its primary dose-response endpoint on CDR-SB at week seventy-six. Shares experienced a notable decline on May 14th. Despite the miss, Biogen is pushing diranersen into registrational development.

MAYA: Two neuro program failures in the same month for a company that built its entire identity around brain disease. Advancing diranersen after a Phase 2 miss is either deep conviction in the tau biology or a reflection of how few strategic alternatives Biogen has left. Either way, the burden of proof on that registrational bet just got substantially heavier.

### [REGULATORY WATCH]

ALEX: Maya, the regulatory calendar was busy in its own right last week.

MAYA: On the regulatory front — two EU milestones from Friday. European regulators recommended approval of both Novo Nordisk's oral Wegovy and AstraZeneca's Etcamah. Separately, AZ and Daiichi Sankyo's Datroway won EU approval in first-line triple-negative breast cancer.

ALEX: Oral Wegovy is the one with the broadest competitive signal. We've tracked the oral GLP-1 landscape extensively on this show. Novo getting EU regulatory backing for an oral semaglutide formulation at obesity doses intensifies the European competitive dynamic — even as Lilly's Foundayo dominates the US conversation.

MAYA: Datroway in first-line TNBC is the one oncologists should sit with. Moving an antibody-drug conjugate to the front of the treatment algorithm in triple-negative breast cancer — a setting that has been anchored to chemotherapy for decades — doesn't just add a new option. It restructures sequencing decisions and formulary positioning from the top down. Every regimen that currently follows chemo gets repositioned.

ALEX: Also worth noting on the regulatory side — Eisai and Biogen's Leqembi autoinjector just had its PDUFA date extended from May 24th to August 24th. That three-month push signals FDA is taking additional time to review the device-drug combination, which delays a meaningful convenience upgrade for Alzheimer's patients and caregivers.

MAYA: And on the workforce side — AbbVie is cutting eighty-five positions at its legacy Allergan site in Irvine, California, effective July 20th per a WARN notice. Novartis is also trimming US headcount. Small numbers in isolation, but years into the Allergan integration, AbbVie is still right-sizing. Every BD team modeling synergies for their next acquisition should note that the headcount optimization tail runs longer than the deal model assumes.
