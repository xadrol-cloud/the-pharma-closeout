### [TOP STORY: PFIZER BRAFTOVI FULL FDA APPROVAL]

ALEX: Let's start with Monday's headline. The FDA granted full approval to Pfizer's Braftovi — encorafenib — in combination with cetuximab and fluorouracil-based chemotherapy for first-line treatment of BRAF V600E-mutant metastatic colorectal cancer. This converts a December 2024 accelerated approval into a traditional full approval, backed by Phase 3 BREAKWATER trial data: fifty-one percent reduction in risk of death versus standard chemotherapy, and median overall survival of 30.3 months compared to 15.1 months.

MAYA: OK, let's unpack that OS number. In first-line metastatic CRC, moving the needle on overall survival is genuinely difficult — the bar is not low. Thirty months versus fifteen months is not a marginal improvement. That is a different conversation you have with a patient about the next two years of their life.

ALEX: And to be precise about who we're talking about — BRAF V600E mutations occur in roughly ten to fifteen percent of metastatic CRC patients. Historically, this population has disproportionately poor prognosis. Their disease is more aggressive, they respond worse to standard chemotherapy. This approval is built specifically for them.

MAYA: Which is exactly why the clinical significance here is real. This isn't a marginal improvement on an already-good baseline. These are patients who historically had very few good options.

ALEX: The commercial picture here is interesting in a few ways. BRAF V600E is a biomarker-selected population, which means payers can draw a clean line — and Pfizer can price to a defined indication with strong clinical justification. But the combination requires cetuximab, which is Lilly's Erbitux. You're looking at a multi-drug regimen where Pfizer and Lilly's products have to work in parallel. That commercial dependency is real.

MAYA: What I'll be watching is how the NCCN guidelines respond. Full approval versus accelerated approval matters in formulary and treatment pathway decisions. On accelerated approval, academic centers lead. With full approval, community oncologists follow. That's where the patient volume is. The label language coming out of this approval will be the real story for how fast this actually gets used.

ALEX: And for Pfizer specifically — this is a company that has been under commercial pressure to demonstrate its oncology pipeline can deliver after the post-COVID reset. A full approval with Phase 3 data showing an OS advantage like this is exactly the kind of result that tells the street the pipeline has real assets in it. Not a perfect story — the combination is complex — but a meaningful one.

MAYA: One more thing people should know: the companion diagnostic matters here. BRAF V600E is detected by molecular testing, and testing rates in community oncology are not uniformly high. The speed at which this gets into practice in non-academic centers will depend partly on how reliably patients are getting biomarker tested upfront. That's a real-world hurdle the clinical data doesn't capture.

### [DEAL & PIPELINE ROUNDUP]

ALEX: Shifting to the deal landscape, and this was a busy week. The lead story is GSK. On Tuesday, GSK announced it will acquire 35Pharma, a Canadian clinical-stage company, for $950 million in cash. The asset is HS235, a potential best-in-class investigational medicine for pulmonary hypertension. GSK cited market forecasts showing the PH market growing to $18 billion by 2032, with activin-pathway inhibitors representing roughly half that revenue. Let's bring in Marcus Webb on this one.

MARCUS: Thanks, Alex. The right frame for this deal is the Acceleron precedent. In 2021, Merck paid $11.5 billion for Acceleron to get sotatercept — now approved and on the market as Winrevair in pulmonary arterial hypertension. Merck bought a late-stage, near-approval asset at a significant premium. GSK is buying a Phase 1 compound for $950 million. Completely different risk profile. But if HS235 delivers on its clinical promise, GSK has entered one of the most commercially attractive cardiovascular markets at roughly eight cents on the dollar compared to what Merck paid. Two things in the deal structure are worth noting. First, HS235 is an activin signaling inhibitor — the same mechanistic class as sotatercept. GSK isn't hedging on mechanism; they're betting they can compete head-to-head with Merck in the same pathway with a next-generation compound. Second, this is Luke Miels' first major acquisition as GSK's CEO. Management tends to plant a flag with their first deal. This one says GSK is serious about cardiovascular. Watch how they structure the Phase 2 program — that will tell you whether they believe in the asset or bought it for optionality.

ALEX: That precedent is exactly the right frame. Merck paid a premium for a validated, late-stage asset. GSK is taking the earlier-stage risk at a dramatically lower entry price. The question is whether HS235 can differentiate from sotatercept on the label — durability, safety profile, dosing convenience.

MAYA: Mechanistically, the activin pathway has been validated by sotatercept's real-world performance. So GSK isn't going off-road on biology. What they need to show is a clinical differentiation story in a patient population Winrevair doesn't cover well, or an adverse event profile that makes switching worthwhile. PH-HFpEF — pulmonary hypertension associated with heart failure with preserved ejection fraction — is one indication where sotatercept's label is limited. If HS235 can demonstrate efficacy there, that's a distinct commercial opportunity.

ALEX: Two more deals to flag quickly. Novo Nordisk struck a $2.1 billion collaboration with Vivtex on Tuesday to develop next-generation oral medicines for obesity and diabetes — Novo continuing to invest heavily in the oral GLP-1 platform alongside semaglutide tablets. And Astellas entered a deal with Vir Biotechnology worth up to $1.4 billion — $335 million upfront including an equity investment — for VIR-5500, a PSMA-targeted T-cell engager in Phase 1 for prostate cancer. Three hundred thirty-five million upfront for a Phase 1 asset is aggressive. Watch that program as early clinical data matures.

### [REGULATORY WATCH]

MAYA: Two more FDA decisions this week that deserve their own conversation. First, Monday: the FDA approved dupilumab — Dupixent — as the first and only approved medicine for allergic fungal rhinosinusitis, or AFRS. This is a distinct, serious type of chronic sinus inflammation triggered by an allergic reaction to fungi growing in the sinuses. It affects up to eight percent of people with chronic sinus disease and nasal polyps, and it's more prevalent in warm, humid regions.

ALEX: And historically, what were the treatment options for these patients?

MAYA: Surgery. And then surgery again when it came back. The Phase 3 data tells the story: fifty percent improvement in sinus opacification with Dupixent versus ten percent with placebo, and a ninety-two percent reduction in the risk of repeat surgeries. That last number is the clinically meaningful one — these patients have been stuck in a cycle of intervention with no pharmacologic way out. Until now.

ALEX: Dupixent keeps expanding. By my count this is somewhere around the ninth approved indication for dupilumab. Sanofi and Regeneron have systematically worked through the entire IL-4/IL-13 disease landscape — atopic dermatitis, asthma, COPD, chronic sinusitis with nasal polyps, eosinophilic esophagitis, prurigo nodularis, and now AFRS. Each new approval extends the commercial runway on a drug they already make and already understand. The marginal cost of each new indication, relative to revenue potential, is very favorable for both companies.

MAYA: Here's what I think people are missing in that story. AFRS isn't just another sinus indication tacked on — it's a mechanistically distinct pathology. The fungal allergy mechanism means this isn't the same patient you're treating with dupilumab for standard chronic rhinosinusitis. The fact that an IL-4/IL-13 blockade works in a fungal-driven allergic disease tells us something real about the inflammatory biology. It may point the way toward other fungal-triggered allergic conditions that haven't been studied yet under this mechanism. The science here is more interesting than the label expansion story suggests.

ALEX: And then Thursday brought another FDA decision. Ascendis Pharma received accelerated approval for navepegritide — brand name YUVIWEL — for achondroplasia in children two years and older. This is the first once-weekly treatment for achondroplasia. The prior approved therapy required daily injections.

MAYA: The once-weekly dosing is not a minor convenience improvement. Achondroplasia is the most common form of short-limb dwarfism, it's a lifelong condition beginning in early childhood, and for a parent managing daily injections for a young child, moving to a weekly schedule is a genuinely meaningful quality-of-life change. The approval came under accelerated approval, so Ascendis still needs to deliver confirmatory data — but the package included three randomized placebo-controlled trials and up to three years of open-label extension data, which is a reasonably robust accelerated approval submission.

ALEX: Worth adding: Ascendis also received a Rare Pediatric Disease Priority Review Voucher with this approval. Those vouchers have sold for north of $100 million in recent transactions — sometimes significantly more. So alongside the clinical win, Ascendis now holds an asset with real cash value. Whether they sell it or retain it for their own pipeline is a business decision worth watching.

### [WHAT TO WATCH]

ALEX: Looking ahead. The in vivo CAR-T space is a thread worth following. Lilly's acquisition of Orna Therapeutics for $2.4 billion — a company developing circular RNA-based in vivo CAR-T for autoimmune disease — continues a clear industry pattern. AbbVie, Bristol Myers Squibb, now Lilly. The hypothesis is that you can instruct a patient's own immune system to generate CAR-T cells using engineered RNA, skipping the manufacturing complexity of traditional cell therapy entirely.

MAYA: The biology is compelling, but all of these programs are Phase 1 at best. The clinical readouts over the next eighteen months will tell us whether the hypothesis holds in patients.

ALEX: Right — and the capital commitments from three of the top ten pharma companies in the same twelve-month window suggest they're not waiting for proof of concept before placing their bets. That's the story to watch. And closer to today: circle back to the NCCN guideline update on Braftovi. That's the mechanism by which a Phase 3 win becomes actual treatment decisions at community oncology centers. Months away, but important.

MAYA: That's the real-world test. Full approval opens the door. Guidelines decide who walks through it.
